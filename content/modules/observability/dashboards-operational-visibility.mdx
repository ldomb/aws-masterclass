## Introduction

Running applications on local devices is straightforward—we can see if they're powered on, check network connectivity, and watch for warnings like low disk space. The device's speed and responsiveness provides immediate feedback about resource availability. However, when services run in the cloud across hundreds or thousands of servers, containers, or serverless environments distributed globally across multiple Availability Zones and Regions, this direct visibility disappears entirely.

At Amazon, **dashboards** serve as the human-facing interface into distributed systems, providing concise summaries of system behavior through time series metrics, logs, traces, and alarms. While automated monitoring and remediation handle the vast majority of issues at scale, humans still need to see what's happening for troubleshooting, validation, capacity planning, and business reviews.

**Dashboarding**—the creation, usage, and ongoing maintenance of dashboards—has evolved into a first-class activity at Amazon, as critical to service success as designing, coding, testing, and deploying. This lesson explores how Amazon designs dashboards for different audiences, applies consistent design principles to maximize usability, and maintains dashboards as living artifacts that evolve with services.

> "Dashboarding has evolved into a first-class activity because it's as critical to the success of our services as other day-to-day software delivery and operational activities."

## The Role of Dashboards in Operations

Amazon doesn't expect operators to monitor dashboards continuously. In fact, operational processes requiring manual dashboard reviews fail due to human error, regardless of review frequency. This isn't a matter of diligence or attention—it's a fundamental limitation of human cognition when faced with continuous monitoring tasks.

Instead, **automated alarms** constantly evaluate critical monitoring data, detecting when systems approach limits (proactive detection, before customer impact) or are already impaired (reactive detection, after impact begins).

### How Alarms and Dashboards Work Together

When alarms trigger, they serve multiple coordinated purposes:

1. **Execute automated remediation workflows** to resolve common issues without human intervention (traffic shifting, instance replacement, cache warming)
2. **Notify operators** with direct links to relevant dashboards and runbooks
3. **Provide validation context** so operators can monitor automated remediation progress and confirm effectiveness
4. **Enable manual intervention** when automation reaches safety-critical checkpoints requiring human confirmation

### Dashboard Use During Operational Events

During operational events, Amazon typically engages multiple on-call operators. Each uses different dashboards as they step through task sequences:

**Impact Quantification**: Dashboards showing how many customers are affected, which customer segments experience the most impact, and the geographic distribution of issues

**Root Cause Triage**: Dashboards tracing across multiple services to identify the source of problems, examining dependency behavior and correlation patterns

**Remediation Monitoring**: Dashboards observing automated remediation workflows in action, showing their effects on system behavior and customer experience

**Mitigation Validation**: Dashboards confirming that runbook-based manual steps are producing expected results

Meanwhile, peer teams and business stakeholders use their own dashboards to monitor ongoing impact. These different participants communicate using incident management tools, chat rooms (with bots like AWS Chatbot), and conference calls. Each stakeholder brings a different perspective on the data they see in dashboards.

<Callout type="info">
The key principle: dashboards exist to serve specific audiences for specific purposes. Amazon works backwards from dashboard users, designing each view based on who will use it and why.
</Callout>

### Beyond Incident Response

Beyond incident response, Amazon uses dashboards for:

#### Weekly Operations Reviews

Each week, Amazon teams and broader organizations run operations review meetings attended by senior leaders, managers, and engineers. During these meetings, they use a "wheel of fortune" to randomly choose high-level audit dashboards. Stakeholders review customer experience and key service-level objectives like availability and latency. The audit dashboards typically show operational data from all Availability Zones and Regions.

#### Long-Term Capacity Planning

When doing capacity planning and forecasting, Amazon uses dashboards that visualize the highest-level business, usage, and capacity metrics over longer time intervals (weeks, months, quarters). These dashboards help teams understand growth trends and plan infrastructure scaling decisions.

#### Deployment Validation

Automated deployment pipelines deploy changes to pre-production environments (alpha, beta, gamma) first. Teams must be able to easily validate changes in these test environments using associated dashboards and automated alarming in a manner exactly consistent with how they'll be validated in production.

### High-Level Dashboards

#### Customer Experience Dashboards

**Customer Experience Dashboards** are Amazon's most important and widely used dashboards. Designed for broad audiences including operators, managers, senior leaders, and stakeholders, they efficiently present overall service health and adherence to goals.

These dashboards display:

**Service-sourced metrics**: Direct measurements from the service itself (request rates, latency percentiles, error counts)

**Client instrumentation**: AWS SDK metrics showing how clients perceive service behavior, continuous tester results from CloudWatch Synthetics canaries that simulate real customer interactions

**Automated remediation status**: What automated systems are doing to address issues, progress indicators, recent actions taken

**Impact metrics**: Number of affected customers, most impacted customer segments (enterprise vs. startup, different geographic regions), depth and breadth of impact

**Time-based context**: Current metrics compared to same time yesterday, last week, normal baseline patterns

These dashboards answer the critical question: **"How are our customers experiencing our service right now?"**

#### System-Level Dashboards

**System-Level Dashboards** provide entry points for understanding how customer-facing endpoints behave. For web-based services with UI and API endpoints, these dashboards display three categories of data for each API operation:

**1. Input Monitoring**
- Request counts (successful, failed, throttled)
- Work polled from queues/streams
- Request size percentiles (p50, p90, p99)
- Authentication and authorization failure counts
- Request validation error breakdowns

**2. Processing Monitoring**
- Business logic path execution counts (which code paths are being exercised)
- Backend microservice request metrics (latency, error rates for each downstream call)
- Fault and error log output (structured error categories)
- Request trace data (end-to-end request flows)
- Cache hit/miss ratios
- Database query execution times

**3. Output Monitoring**
- Response type counts with error/fault response breakdowns by category
- Response size percentiles
- Time-to-first-byte latency (TTFB)
- Time-to-complete-response latency
- Bandwidth utilization

<Callout type="warning">
Amazon deliberately avoids adding too many metrics to high-level dashboards. Information overload distracts from the core message these dashboards must convey. If a dashboard becomes cluttered, it's a signal to create specialized drill-down dashboards instead.
</Callout>

#### Service Instance Dashboards

**Service Instance Dashboards** facilitate fast and comprehensive evaluation of customer experience within a single service instance (partition or cell). This narrow view ensures operators working on specific instances aren't overwhelmed with irrelevant data from other instances.

For example, a global service might have:
- US-East-1 Instance Dashboard
- EU-West-1 Instance Dashboard
- AP-Southeast-1 Instance Dashboard

Each shows only the metrics relevant to that geographic instance, allowing focused troubleshooting without noise from healthy instances.

#### Service Audit Dashboards

**Service Audit Dashboards** intentionally display data for ALL instances of a service across all Availability Zones and Regions. These dashboards serve two critical purposes:

1. **Operations Meeting Reviews**: Used during weekly operations meetings where senior leaders audit service health across the entire footprint
2. **Alarm Coverage Verification**: Operators verify that automated alarming is properly configured and functioning across all service instances

These dashboards reveal patterns like:
- One region performing differently than others
- Gradual degradation across all regions (indicating a global issue)
- Alarm configuration drift (where some instances have proper alarming, others don't)

#### Capacity Planning and Forecasting Dashboards

**Capacity Planning Dashboards** visualize long-term growth trends with metrics aggregated over extended periods. These dashboards help teams:

- Understand historical growth patterns
- Forecast future capacity needs
- Plan hardware procurement timelines
- Identify scaling bottlenecks before they cause issues
- Justify infrastructure investments to leadership

Typical time ranges include:
- 3 months of daily aggregates (90 datapoints)
- 9 months of daily aggregates (270 datapoints)
- 15 months of daily aggregates (450 datapoints) for year-over-year comparisons

*Your e-commerce service has customer experience, system-level, and capacity planning dashboards. During an incident, which do you check first? During a quarterly business review presentation? During annual budget planning? Why does the order change?*

### Low-Level Dashboards

While high-level dashboards provide broad overviews, low-level dashboards dive deep into specific components. Amazon APIs typically orchestrate requests across backend microservices owned by different teams. Each team builds specialized dashboards for their specific components.

#### Microservice-Specific Dashboards

**Microservice-Specific Dashboards** show implementation details requiring deep service knowledge. These dashboards are primarily used by the teams that own the services. Key characteristics:

**Aggregated presentation**: Services are heavily instrumented, but dashboards present data in aggregated form to avoid overwhelming operators

**Drill-down capability**: When operators identify anomalies in aggregated data, they use additional tools for ad-hoc queries that de-aggregate data, trace requests, and reveal correlations

**Implementation specifics**: Metrics like connection pool utilization, thread pool depths, garbage collection times, cache eviction rates, circuit breaker states

**Per-API granularity**: Separate metrics for each API operation the microservice exposes

Example microservice dashboard sections:
- Request processing metrics (latency by API, error categories)
- Resource utilization (connection pools, thread pools, memory)
- Dependency interactions (calls to downstream services)
- Cache performance (hit rates, eviction patterns, memory usage)
- Queue depths (inbound request queues, outbound callback queues)

#### Infrastructure Dashboards

**Infrastructure Dashboards** focus on compute resource metrics from the infrastructure services the systems run on:

**EC2 Instances**:
- CPU utilization (user, system, iowait)
- Network traffic (bytes in/out, packets in/out)
- Disk I/O (read/write operations, throughput)
- Space utilization (disk full warnings)
- Instance status checks

**ECS/EKS Containers**:
- Container CPU and memory utilization
- Container restart counts
- Pod scheduling latency
- Cluster-level resource availability

**AWS Lambda Functions**:
- Invocation counts and error rates
- Duration percentiles
- Concurrent executions
- Throttling counts
- Cold start frequencies

**Related Cluster Metrics**:
- Auto Scaling group size (desired, current, minimum, maximum)
- Auto Scaling activities (scale-out, scale-in events)
- Quota utilization (how close to AWS service limits)

#### Dependency Dashboards

**Dependency Dashboards** provide views of how both upstream dependencies (proxies, load balancers) and downstream dependencies (data stores, queues, streams) behave **as measured by the microservice**.

Even if the teams that own those dependencies already have their own dashboards, each microservice owner typically creates dedicated dependency dashboards because:

1. **Client-side perspective**: Measures what the microservice actually experiences, not just what the dependency reports
2. **Network path visibility**: Captures issues in the network path between services
3. **Client configuration issues**: Detects problems with timeouts, connection pools, retry logic
4. **Correlation with own metrics**: Makes it easy to correlate dependency behavior with microservice performance

These dashboards also track critical non-functional aspects:
- Security certificate expiry dates
- Dependency quota usage
- API version compatibility
- Connection pool health

## Dashboard Design Principles

Consistency in data presentation is critical to successful dashboarding. Amazon has identified and refined design conventions that make dashboards accessible to the broadest audience, increasing organizational value.

### Why Design Consistency Matters

A very common tendency when designing dashboards is to overestimate or underestimate the target user's domain knowledge. It's easy to build a dashboard that makes total sense to its creator but provides no value to users. Amazon uses the technique of **working backwards from the customer** (in this case, the dashboard users) to eliminate this risk.

Amazon has even found subtle ways to measure and improve design conventions over time. For example, if a new operator can quickly understand and use the data presented in dashboards to learn how a service works, that's an indication those dashboards are presenting the right information in the right way.

### Layout and Organization

#### Most Important Data at the Top

Dashboards render from top to bottom, and users tend to interpret the initially rendered graphs (visible when the dashboard loads) as the most important. Amazon's design convention places the most important data at the top of dashboards.

For web services, aggregated/summary availability graphs and end-to-end latency percentile graphs typically belong at the top. These immediately answer: "Is the service healthy?" and "Are customers getting good response times?"

<Callout type="tip">
Think of your dashboard as a newspaper front page. The most critical "above-the-fold" content should be immediately visible without scrolling. Bury implementation details "below the fold" for when operators need to dig deeper.
</Callout>

#### Size Communicates Importance

Amazon uses larger graphs for the most important metrics. This visual hierarchy helps operators quickly identify what matters most. Guidelines:

- **Largest graphs** (full width): Overall availability, end-to-end latency
- **Medium graphs** (half width): Per-API metrics, dependency health
- **Small graphs** (quarter width): Supporting metrics, detailed breakdowns

If graphs have many metrics, ensure legends don't vertically or horizontally squeeze visible graph data. For search queries that might return many metrics, allow for larger than normal result sets.

#### Avoid Horizontal Scrolling

Amazon lays out graphs for the expected minimum display resolution, avoiding forced horizontal scrolling. An on-call operator on a laptop at 3 AM might not notice the horizontal scroll bar without obvious visual cues. Missing critical data due to hidden horizontal content can significantly delay incident resolution.

Typical minimum resolution target: 1366×768 (common laptop screen size)

#### Display Time Zones Prominently

For dashboards displaying date and time data, Amazon ensures the related time zone is visible. For dashboards used concurrently by operators in different time zones, Amazon defaults to one time zone (UTC) that all users can relate to.

This allows users to communicate with each other using a single time reference, saving time and effort from excessive mental time zone translations. During incident calls, operators can say "the spike at 02:15 UTC" and everyone knows exactly what timeframe to examine.

### Time Intervals and Resolution

#### Default to Common Use Cases

Amazon defaults to time intervals and datapoint periods relevant to the most common use cases. All graphs on a dashboard initially display data for the same time range and resolution.

For operational triage (the most common use case), Amazon finds that:
- **3-hour interval** with **1-minute resolution** (180 datapoints)
- Renders clearly even in small dashboard widgets
- Provides sufficient context to understand patterns leading to current state
- Loads quickly even on slower connections

#### Benefits of All Graphs Sharing Time Range

When all graphs within a dashboard section have the same horizontal size and time range:
- Easy time correlation between graphs
- Visual alignment helps spot causal relationships
- Operators can draw mental vertical lines across graphs

#### Avoid Excessive Datapoints

Amazon avoids plotting too many datapoints in graphs because:

1. **Performance**: Slows down dashboard load time
2. **Visualization**: Excessive datapoints actually reduce visibility into anomalies
3. **Cognitive load**: Too much detail makes patterns harder to see

Example: A graph showing 3 hours at 1-minute resolution (180 values) renders clearly. But 3 hours at 1-second resolution (10,800 values) becomes an illegible dense line that obscures patterns.

#### Enable Quick Adjustments

Amazon dashboards provide controls for quickly adjusting both time interval and metric period for all graphs simultaneously. Common interval × resolution ratios:

| Use Case | Interval | Resolution | Datapoints |
|----------|----------|------------|------------|
| Ongoing event zoom | 1 hour | 1 minute | 60 |
| Current troubleshooting | 3 hours | 1 minute | 180 |
| Extended investigation | 12 hours | 1 minute | 720 |
| Daily trends | 1 day | 5 minutes | 288 |
| Multi-day patterns | 3 days | 5 minutes | 864 |
| Weekly trends | 1 week | 1 hour | 168 |
| Monthly overview | 1 month | 1 hour | 744 |
| Quarterly trends | 3 months | 1 day | 90 |
| Long-term capacity | 15 months | 1 day | 450 |

### Graph Annotations and Clarity

#### Annotate with Alarm Thresholds

When graphing metrics that have related automated alarms, Amazon uses visual annotations:

**Static thresholds**: Horizontal lines showing where alarms trigger

**Dynamic thresholds**: When using AI/ML-based predictions, display both the actual metric and threshold metric in the same graph

**Known limits**: If a metric has known limits (like "maximum tested" load or hard resource limits), annotate graphs with horizontal lines showing these limits

**Goal lines**: For metrics with business or technical goals, add horizontal lines making those goals immediately visible

This visual context helps operators quickly assess:
- How close are we to alarm thresholds?
- Is this spike still below our tested capacity?
- Are we meeting our goals?

#### Avoid Dual Y-Axis Ambiguity

Amazon avoids adding horizontal lines to graphs that use both left and right y-axes. Users might find it difficult to know which y-axis the horizontal line relates to. To avoid this ambiguity, Amazon splits these into two graphs using single y-axes and adds horizontal lines only to the appropriate graph.

```
BAD:
┌─────────────────┐
│ ▲ (left)   ▲ (right)
│ │          │
│ ----Line---- ← Which axis?
│ │          │
└─────────────────┘

GOOD:
┌─────────────────┐
│ ▲ (left)
│ │
│ ----Line----
│ │
└─────────────────┘
┌─────────────────┐
│ ▲ (right)
│ │ ----Line----
│ │
└─────────────────┘
```

#### Match Y-Axis to Metric Ranges

Amazon avoids overloading y-axes with metrics having very disparate value ranges. For example, plotting p0 (minimum) and p100 (maximum) latencies on the same graph where p100 values may be orders of magnitude larger than p0 reduces visibility into the variance of one or more metrics.

Bad example: p0 latencies range 1-5ms, p100 latencies range 500-5000ms. On the same graph, the p0 line appears flat and conveys no information.

Better approach: Separate graphs for:
- p10, Average, p90 latencies
- p99.9 and Maximum (p100) latencies

#### Be Wary of Auto-Ranging Y-Axes

Limiting y-axis range to current datapoint values only can make a metric look far more variable than it actually is at a casual glance.

Example: CPU utilization ranges from 45% to 47% (very stable), but if the y-axis auto-scales to 45-47% range, the graph shows dramatic-looking spikes. An operator glancing at it might think CPU is highly variable when it's actually extremely stable.

Better approach: Set meaningful y-axis ranges (like 0-100% for percentages, or 0-maximum capacity for throughput).

### Graph Content Best Practices

#### Avoid Overloading Single Graphs

Amazon ensures dashboards don't have too many statistics or unrelated metrics in a single graph. For request processing, Amazon typically creates separate adjacent graphs:

**Graph 1 - Availability**
- Availability % (Success Rate = (Total - Faults) / Total × 100)
- Threshold line at 99.9% or other SLA target

**Graph 2 - Typical Latencies**
- p10 latency
- Average latency
- p90 latency

**Graph 3 - High Percentile Latencies**
- p99 latency
- p99.9 latency
- Maximum (p100) latency

This separation prevents the common problem where high maximum latencies compress all other percentiles into illegibility at the bottom of the graph.

#### Provide Context for All Metrics

Amazon doesn't assume users know exactly what every metric or widget means, especially for implementation-specific metrics. Dashboards provide sufficient context through:

**Description text** beside or below each graph explaining:
- What the metric measures
- What "normal" looks like
- What it might mean if the graph isn't "normal"
- What actions to take if values are concerning

**Links to resources**:
- Runbooks for common scenarios
- Related "dive deep" dashboards for more detail
- Equivalent dashboards for other clusters/partitions
- Deployment pipelines to see recent changes
- Contact information for dependency owners

For subject-matter experts, the dashboard itself can serve as the runbook when it has sufficient inline context.

#### Use Appropriate Widget Types

Depending on dashboard use cases, Amazon finds that displaying:
- A **single number** (latest metric value)
- An **alarm status widget**
- A **simple stat comparison** (current vs. yesterday)

...is sometimes more appropriate than displaying a complex time series graph of all recent datapoints.

Example: For a capacity planning dashboard reviewed weekly, showing "Current Active Users: 1.2M (+15% vs last week)" is clearer than a detailed time series.

#### Avoid Sparse Metrics

**Sparse metrics** are metrics only emitted when certain error conditions exist. While efficient to implement, sparse metrics confuse dashboard users with empty or almost empty graphs.

Problem: Empty graph could mean:
1. No errors (good!)
2. Metric emission is broken (bad!)
3. Data collection pipeline has issues (bad!)

Solution: Modify services to continually emit safe (zero) values for these metrics in the absence of error conditions. Operators can then easily understand that absence of data implies the service isn't emitting telemetry correctly, not that errors aren't happening.

```python
# BAD: Only emit errors
if error_count > 0:
    metrics.emit("Errors", error_count)

# GOOD: Always emit, even if zero
metrics.emit("Errors", error_count)  # Emits 0 when no errors
```

#### Add Per-Mode Metrics for Multi-Modal Behavior

When displaying graphs for metrics that aggregate multi-modal behavior, Amazon adds separate graphs showing per-mode metrics.

**Example 1 - Variable-sized requests**: If a service supports variable-sized requests, create:
- Overall latency graph (all requests)
- Small request latency graph (< 1KB)
- Medium request latency graph (1KB - 1MB)
- Large request latency graph (> 1MB)

**Example 2 - Variable execution paths**: If execution varies by input parameters, create separate metrics capturing each mode:
- Cached read path
- Database read path
- Batch operation path
- Single-item operation path

This prevents the problem where slow large requests make average latency look bad, obscuring the fact that small requests (which might be 99% of traffic) are performing well.

## Dashboard Maintenance

Building dashboards is only the first step. As systems continually evolve and scale, dashboards must evolve alongside them. Amazon has learned that dashboard maintenance is not optional overhead—it's essential to keeping dashboards valuable.

### Continuous Improvement Culture

Before completing code changes and during code reviews, Amazon developers ask: **"Do I need to update any dashboards?"** This question is as important as "Did I write tests?" or "Did I update documentation?"

Developers are empowered to make dashboard changes before deploying underlying code changes. This avoids situations where operators must update dashboards during or after deployments to validate the changes being deployed—potentially missing critical issues during the most risky time.

### Signs a Dashboard Needs Refinement

**Excessive detail**: If a dashboard contains far more detailed information than typical dashboards, it might indicate operators are relying on it for manual anomaly detection instead of using automated alarming. Amazon continuously audits dashboards to identify opportunities for:
- Improving service instrumentation
- Enhancing automated alarming
- Reducing manual effort

**Stale graphs**: Amazon aggressively prunes or updates graphs that no longer add value to dashboards. Signs of staleness:
- Metric hasn't changed in months
- Business logic it measured was removed
- Better alternative metric now available
- No one remembers why it's there

**Dashboard proliferation**: Too many dashboards can be as bad as too few. Amazon periodically reviews dashboard libraries to consolidate redundant dashboards and ensure each has a clear purpose and audience.

### Infrastructure as Code

Amazon follows the **Infrastructure-as-Code (IaC)** process for maintaining dashboards. This ensures dashboards are:

**Version controlled**: Maintained in Git or similar version control systems

**Code-reviewed**: Dashboard changes go through the same review process as code changes

**Automatically deployed**: Deployed using the same tools developers and operators use for services (like AWS CDK, CloudFormation, or Terraform)

**Environment-consistent**: Complete, identical dashboards exist for pre-production environments (alpha, beta, gamma)

Benefits of IaC for dashboards:

1. **Consistency**: Automated deployment pipelines deploy changes to pre-production first, then production
2. **Reviewability**: Changes are reviewed before deployment
3. **Rollback capability**: Bad dashboard changes can be reverted
4. **Audit trail**: Complete history of who changed what and when
5. **Reusability**: Dashboard templates can be reused across similar services

### Post-Incident Reviews

After unexpected operational events, Amazon teams review whether dashboard improvements could have:

**Preempted the event**: Would better dashboards have revealed the problem earlier through earlier detection?

**Identified root causes faster**: Did dashboards clearly show what was wrong, or did operators waste time hunting for data?

**Reduced mean time to recovery**: Did dashboards help operators quickly confirm mitigation effectiveness?

If the answer to any of these questions is "no," post-mortems include specific actions to refine dashboards.

Post-incident questions to ask:

1. Did the dashboards clearly show customer impact?
2. Did the dashboards help operators triangulate to determine root cause?
3. Did the dashboards assist with measuring time to recovery?
4. Were there key metrics missing that would have helped?
5. Were there metrics present that distracted from the actual problem?
6. Did graph annotations (thresholds, goals) provide helpful context?

## CloudWatch Application Signals: Automatic Baseline Visibility

While the manual dashboard practices described throughout this lesson remain essential for thoughtful operational visibility, AWS CloudWatch Application Signals (introduced in 2025) now provides automatic baseline observability that complements these practices.

### Automatic Discovery and Visualization

CloudWatch Application Signals automatically discovers and visualizes your application topology without requiring manual instrumentation. This is particularly valuable for teams getting started with observability or for rapidly evolving microservice architectures where manual dashboard maintenance can lag behind architectural changes.

For services running on Amazon EKS (Elastic Kubernetes Service), Application Signals provides auto-collected metrics and traces with a single configuration flag. The service automatically instruments your applications to collect:

- Service-to-service dependency maps
- Request rates, error rates, and latency percentiles for each service
- Distributed traces showing complete request flows across services
- Correlation between metrics and traces for faster troubleshooting

### Built-In SLO Monitoring

Application Signals includes built-in dashboards for SLO (Service Level Objective) monitoring. These dashboards automatically track availability and latency against configured targets, making it easier to understand whether your service is meeting its reliability goals without building custom availability calculations.

### Cross-Account Observability

For organizations with multiple AWS accounts, Application Signals supports cross-account observability, allowing you to build unified monitoring views across your entire infrastructure. This addresses a common challenge where services span multiple accounts but operators need a single view for troubleshooting.

### Relationship to Manual Dashboards

Application Signals provides automatic baseline visibility, but the principles in this lesson remain critical. Amazon teams still build purpose-designed dashboards for:

- **Business-specific metrics**: Metrics unique to your domain that automatic instrumentation cannot capture
- **Customized operational views**: Layouts and prioritization tailored to your team's specific workflows
- **Advanced troubleshooting scenarios**: Deep-dive dashboards with implementation-specific metrics
- **Cross-service business flows**: End-to-end visibility across multiple services from a business process perspective

Think of Application Signals as providing the foundation layer of observability—ensuring you have basic visibility without manual effort—while custom dashboards provide the specialized, context-aware views that make your operational processes efficient.

<KeyTakeaways>
- **Design dashboards for specific audiences**: Work backwards from dashboard users to determine what data to present and how. A dashboard designed for senior leaders looks completely different from one designed for on-call engineers
- **Layer dashboards by perspective**: High-level dashboards for customer experience and auditing provide breadth; low-level dashboards for implementation details and dependencies provide depth when troubleshooting
- **Prioritize consistency and clarity**: Use standardized layouts, appropriate time intervals, clear annotations, and contextual descriptions to maximize usability. Consistency across dashboards reduces cognitive load during incidents
- **Maintain dashboards as code**: Treat dashboards as living artifacts that evolve with services through IaC practices, version control, and code review. Stale dashboards are worse than no dashboards
- **Complement automation, don't replace it**: Dashboards support incident response and validation but should never be the primary detection mechanism—automated alarms handle that role. Manual monitoring always fails eventually
- **Iterate based on real use**: Post-incident reviews should always ask whether dashboards helped or hindered. The best dashboards evolve from real operational experience, not upfront design
</KeyTakeaways>
