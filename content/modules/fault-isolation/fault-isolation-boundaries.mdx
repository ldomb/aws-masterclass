## Introduction

Building highly available systems in the cloud requires a deep understanding of how infrastructure can fail and how to contain those failures. AWS provides multiple layers of fault isolation boundaries that enable you to architect systems that remain resilient even when components fail. These boundaries are not just physical separations of infrastructure—they represent carefully designed abstractions that help you build predictable, reliable systems.

Understanding these fault isolation boundaries is essential for making informed architectural decisions. When you know how AWS services are designed with respect to these boundaries, you can intentionally choose dependencies that align with your availability goals. This knowledge transforms fault isolation from a theoretical concept into a practical tool for building production systems that meet demanding availability targets.

The journey to understanding fault isolation begins with recognizing that not all service failures have the same scope. A single failed server might affect only a small percentage of requests. A failed data center could impact an entire Availability Zone. A regional event could affect multiple zones. By understanding these boundaries and their characteristics, you can make deliberate choices about how your workload uses AWS services to achieve your specific resilience requirements.

**Think About It**: Consider a traditional on-premises data center. What are the fault isolation boundaries? Typically, you might have server racks sharing power distribution, networking equipment serving multiple racks, and a building housing all infrastructure. If the building loses power, everything fails together. AWS's multi-layered approach provides more granular boundaries, but understanding when and how to use them requires careful study.

## The Hierarchy of AWS Fault Isolation

AWS infrastructure is organized into a hierarchy of fault isolation boundaries, each serving a specific purpose in containing failures and enabling resilient architectures. These boundaries build upon each other, creating layers of protection that you can leverage based on your availability requirements.

### Availability Zones: The Foundation of Resilience

At the foundation are **Availability Zones** (AZs), which consist of one or more discrete data centers with redundant power, networking, and connectivity. Each Availability Zone is designed to operate independently, with physical separation (up to 60 miles or 100 km) that protects against correlated failures from events like power outages, natural disasters, or localized infrastructure issues.

AWS operates over 100 Availability Zones across multiple Regions globally. This massive infrastructure deployment enables customers worldwide to build highly available applications. Each zone is engineered with several critical characteristics:

**Physical Independence**: Availability Zones don't share common points of failure like generators, cooling equipment, or power substations. When AWS designs a Region, they ensure that different zones receive power from different substations provided by different utilities when possible. This separation means that a power grid failure affecting one substation doesn't propagate to other zones.

The physical separation distance—up to 60 miles—provides protection against localized disasters. A tornado, earthquake, flood, or fire that impacts one zone's data centers is extremely unlikely to simultaneously affect another zone's facilities. Yet the zones remain close enough for the next critical characteristic.

**High-Speed Interconnection**: All Availability Zones in a Region are connected with high-bandwidth, low-latency networking over fully redundant, dedicated metro fiber. This connectivity provides single-digit millisecond latency between zones, making synchronous replication practical and allowing applications to fail over between zones without noticeable interruption to users.

The network architecture deserves special attention. Each Availability Zone connects to the internet through two transit centers where AWS peers with multiple tier-1 internet providers. This redundant internet connectivity means that even if one transit center or provider experiences issues, zones maintain internet connectivity through alternate paths.

**Deployment Separation**: When AWS deploys updates to services, deployments to Availability Zones in the same Region are separated in time to prevent correlated failures. This temporal separation is just as important as physical separation. A problematic software deployment affecting one zone gets detected and rolled back before deploying to other zones, preventing a bad deployment from taking down an entire Region.

<Callout type="info">
The logical construct of Availability Zones and their connectivity provides a critical balance: independent enough to fail separately, close enough to replicate synchronously. This balance makes them ideal building blocks for highly available architectures within a Region.
</Callout>

### Regions: Geographic Isolation

Above Availability Zones sit **Regions**, which are geographic areas containing multiple Availability Zones. Each Region is isolated and independent from other Regions, with a few specific exceptions for certain global services (which we'll examine in detail later). This isolation means that service failures, when they occur, are typically contained within a single Region. The resources you create in one Region don't exist in other Regions unless you explicitly replicate them.

As of current counts, AWS operates multiple Regions worldwide, with each Region containing at least three Availability Zones. This design choice—requiring at least three zones per Region—enables customers to implement N+1 or N+2 redundancy patterns. With three zones, you can lose one zone and still have two remaining. The minimum of three zones makes architectural patterns like "active-active with 50% overprovisioning" practical for static stability.

Regions themselves vary in size and capacity. Some Regions have significantly more than three zones—the larger, more established Regions often have five or six zones—providing even more flexibility for architectural patterns that require broad distribution.

The separation between Regions extends beyond just physical distance. Each Region operates its own independent infrastructure for most services. The control planes for Regional services operate independently. The data planes operate independently. This independence means that a problem affecting service infrastructure in one Region doesn't propagate to another Region's infrastructure.

**Think About It**: Why does AWS require a minimum of three Availability Zones per Region instead of two? Consider the math of redundancy: with two zones and 50% overprovisioning in each, losing one zone leaves you at exactly 100% capacity—no room for any additional failures or traffic spikes. Three zones with 50% overprovisioning in each means losing one zone still leaves you at 150% of required capacity, providing safety margins.

### Partitions: Compliance and Administrative Boundaries

At the highest level, AWS groups Regions into **Partitions**. Every Region belongs to exactly one partition, and partitions provide hard boundaries between Regions in different environments. This separation isn't just logical—it's fundamental to how AWS operates these distinct environments.

The **aws partition** contains all commercial AWS Regions. When you use the standard AWS console and create resources in us-east-1, eu-west-1, or any of the typical Regions, you're operating in the aws partition.

The **aws-cn partition** contains Regions in China, operated by partners in accordance with local regulations and requirements. These Regions are completely separate from the commercial partition, with independent instances of all services.

The **aws-us-gov partition** contains AWS GovCloud Regions designed to meet US government compliance requirements like FedRAMP and DOD SRG. These Regions operate independently from commercial Regions, with additional security and compliance controls.

Partitions have several critical implications for how you architect systems:

**Independent IAM**: Each partition has its own completely separate instance of AWS Identity and Access Management (IAM). Credentials from one partition cannot access resources in another partition. If you have resources in both aws and aws-us-gov partitions, you need separate IAM users, roles, and policies for each.

**Cross-Region Features Within Partitions**: Services that provide cross-Region functionality—like S3 Cross-Region Replication, Transit Gateway Inter-Region peering, or  replication—only work between Regions in the same partition. You cannot replicate an S3 bucket from us-east-1 (aws partition) to cn-north-1 (aws-cn partition) using Cross-Region Replication.

**Service Availability**: Not all services are available in all partitions. Some services may be available in aws but not in aws-cn or aws-us-gov due to regulatory requirements, compliance needs, or timing of service launches.

## Control Planes and Data Planes: A Critical Distinction

One of the most important patterns in AWS service design is the separation between control planes and data planes. Understanding this distinction is crucial for building resilient systems and making informed architectural decisions.

### What Are Control Planes?

The **control plane** encompasses all the machinery involved in making changes to your infrastructure—creating resources, modifying configurations, deleting resources, and propagating those changes throughout the system to take effect.

Consider what happens when you launch an EC2 instance. The EC2 control plane must:

1. Find suitable physical hardware in the specified Availability Zone that meets your requirements (instance type, placement group constraints, VPC tenancy)
2. Allocate network interfaces from your VPC subnet's available IP addresses
3. Prepare EBS volumes, either creating new volumes or attaching existing ones
4. Generate temporary IAM credentials if the instance uses an IAM role
5. Install and propagate Security Group rules to the appropriate network infrastructure
6. Store metadata in various databases so other services can discover and interact with the instance
7. Propagate configurations to the physical server and network edge devices
8. Coordinate with multiple downstream services to complete the launch

This orchestration is complex, involving numerous systems, databases, and workflows. The control plane must maintain consistency, handle failures gracefully, and coordinate across multiple components.

### What Are Data Planes?

The **data plane**, in contrast, handles the primary function of a service once resources are provisioned. For EC2, the data plane:

- Routes packets according to VPC route tables
- Reads and writes from EBS volumes
- Enforces security group rules on network traffic
- Maintains network connectivity for running instances

These operations are fundamentally simpler than control plane operations. The data plane doesn't need complex orchestration or consensus across multiple systems. The physical machine hosting an EC2 instance has local access to routing information needed to send and receive packets. It can forward traffic, apply security rules, and interact with storage without consulting centralized control systems.

> "Data planes are intentionally less complicated, with fewer moving parts compared to control planes, which usually implement a complex system of workflows, business logic, and databases. This makes failure events statistically less likely to occur in the data plane versus the control plane."

This separation has profound implications for availability. Because data planes are simpler, they can be designed to target higher availability than control planes. More importantly, this enables a critical pattern called **static stability**.

### Static Stability: Operating Without Changes

Static stability means that systems continue operating normally in their current state, even when dependencies become impaired. A statically stable system doesn't need to make changes to recover from failures—it's already prepared for them.

Consider an EC2 instance running in your VPC. Once launched, that instance operates independently of the EC2 control plane. It doesn't need to make API calls to continue running or to reboot. The physical server hosting the instance has local access to all routing information needed to send and receive packets, both inside the VPC and to the internet.

Even if the EC2 control plane becomes impaired and can't launch new instances, existing instances continue operating normally. They route traffic according to VPC route tables, read and write to EBS volumes, and maintain network connectivity—all data plane operations that don't depend on the control plane.

This principle extends throughout AWS service design:

- An **S3 bucket**, once created, serves GET and PUT requests through the data plane regardless of control plane availability
- A **running RDS database** continues processing queries even if you can't modify its configuration
- **VPC networking** continues routing traffic according to existing route tables
- **ELB load balancers** continue distributing traffic to healthy targets
- **Lambda functions** continue executing even if you can't deploy new versions

<Callout type="tip">
The most reliable recovery mechanisms are those that require the fewest changes. By pre-provisioning capacity and avoiding dependencies on control planes during failure scenarios, you create systems that are statically stable and more resilient.
</Callout>

For customers building on AWS, implementing static stability means designing systems that don't depend on control plane operations to recover from failures:

- Instead of launching new instances when an Availability Zone fails, pre-provision enough capacity in other zones to handle the load
- Instead of creating ELBs during an outage, have them already deployed in your disaster recovery Region
- Instead of modifying DNS records to perform failover, use Route 53 health checks to automatically route away from unhealthy endpoints
- Instead of updating IAM policies during an incident, pre-provision "break-glass" users with necessary permissions

This approach trades some additional cost (for pre-provisioned resources) for significantly improved reliability during failure scenarios. The trade-off is worthwhile for production workloads where recovery time objectives (RTOs) matter.

## AWS Service Types and Fault Boundaries

AWS services fall into three categories based on their fault isolation boundaries: zonal, regional, and global. Each type has different characteristics that fundamentally affect how you should use them in your architectures.

### Zonal Services: Maximum Isolation

**Zonal services** like EC2 and EBS allow you to specify which Availability Zone to deploy resources into. These services operate independently in each Availability Zone, with zonal data planes and often zonal control planes. A failure in one zone doesn't affect the service in other zones.

The architecture of a zonal service typically includes:

**Zonal Data Planes**: Each Availability Zone runs its own independent data plane infrastructure. EC2 instances in us-east-1a are served by data plane components that don't depend on us-east-1b's components. This independence means that a data plane failure in one zone is isolated to that zone.

**Zonal Control Planes (for some operations)**: Services like EC2 also implement zonal control planes for zone-specific operations like launching instances. Each zone has its own instance launch workflow, capacity planning systems, and resource allocation infrastructure.

**Regional Control Plane Endpoint**: While zonal control planes handle zone-specific operations, AWS also provides a regional control plane endpoint to simplify interaction. This regional endpoint serves as an aggregation and routing layer on top of the zonal control planes, determining which zone's control plane should handle each request.

When AWS deploys updates to zonal services, deployments to different Availability Zones happen on different days. A deployment to us-east-1a might occur on Monday, us-east-1b on Wednesday, and us-east-1c on Friday. This temporal separation means a problem with a deployment affects one zone, gets detected and rolled back, before ever reaching other zones.

Availability Zones give customers the ability to operate production workloads that are more highly available, fault tolerant, and scalable than would be possible from a single data center. When you use multiple Availability Zones, you're better isolated and protected from issues impacting a single zone's physical infrastructure.

**Designing with Zonal Services**: To take advantage of zonal independence:

- Deploy resources across at least three Availability Zones (enables losing one zone while maintaining redundancy)
- Implement static stability by pre-provisioning capacity to handle zone failures without launching new instances
- Disable cross-zone load balancing on ELB to ensure traffic to one zone doesn't depend on another zone's instances
- Use zonal endpoints for VPC endpoints to prevent cross-zone dependencies
- Consider active-active patterns with overprovisioning (N+1 or N+2 capacity)

Common zonal services include: EC2, EBS, subnet resources in VPC, EC2 Auto Scaling groups (though they span zones, they use zonal EC2 instances), and Elastic Network Interfaces.

### Regional Services: Abstracted Multi-AZ

**Regional services** are services that AWS has built on top of multiple Availability Zones so customers don't have to figure out how to make best use of zonal services themselves. AWS logically groups the service deployed across multiple zones to present a single Regional endpoint.

Amazon SQS and Amazon DynamoDB exemplify regional services. They use the isolation and redundancy of Availability Zones internally to minimize infrastructure failure as a category of availability and durability risk. Amazon S3, for example, spreads requests and data across multiple Availability Zones and is designed to automatically recover from the failure of an Availability Zone. However, you only interact with the Regional endpoint of the service.

From a customer perspective, regional services are simpler to use because you don't need to think about zone distribution, health checks, or failover logic. The service handles all multi-AZ complexity internally. The trade-off is less control over the specifics of data placement and less ability to implement certain advanced resilience patterns.

**How Regional Services Achieve High Availability**: Internal to AWS, regional services typically:

- Run independent stacks of infrastructure in each Availability Zone
- Replicate data across zones (with varying consistency models depending on the service)
- Use consensus algorithms (like Paxos or Raft) to maintain consistency
- Implement automatic failover when a zone experiences issues
- Health-check their own infrastructure and route requests away from impaired zones

For example, DynamoDB automatically replicates data across three Availability Zones. Writes don't complete until they're durable in multiple zones. If one zone becomes unavailable, DynamoDB continues serving requests from the remaining zones without customer intervention.

AWS believes that most customers can achieve their resilience goals in a single Region by using regional services or multi-AZ architectures built with zonal services. The isolation provided by Availability Zones, combined with services designed to use that isolation effectively, provides excellent resilience for most workloads.

**Multi-Region Considerations**: Some workloads require additional redundancy beyond what a single Region provides. You can use the isolation of AWS Regions to create multi-Region architectures for high availability or business continuity purposes. The physical and logical separation between Regions avoids correlated failures.

However, multi-Region architectures introduce significant complexity:

- Data replication across Regions is asynchronous (using AWS-provided services), introducing potential for data loss or inconsistency during failover
- Latency between Regions is higher than within a Region, affecting application performance
- Failover orchestration becomes more complex, often requiring specific ordering of service shutdowns and startups
- Testing multi-Region failover requires significant investment and careful planning
- Costs increase due to data transfer and duplicated infrastructure

Common regional services include: DynamoDB, SQS, SNS, Lambda (though functions run in VPCs span specific zones), S3, ECS (the service is regional, though tasks run in specific zones), and Step Functions.

### Global Services: Special Considerations

A small set of AWS services have control planes and data planes that don't exist independently in each Region. These are commonly referred to as **global services**. Understanding how these services work is crucial for avoiding single points of failure in your architecture.

Global AWS services follow the conventional pattern of separating control plane and data plane to achieve static stability. The significant difference for global services is that their control plane is hosted in a single AWS Region, while their data plane may be globally distributed or regionalized.

There are three types of global services, each with different implications for your architecture:

#### 1. Partitional Services (Global Per Partition)

Some global services exist in each partition with control planes in a single Region per partition. These **partitional services** include:

- **AWS IAM** (control plane in us-east-1 for aws partition)
- **AWS Organizations** (control plane in us-east-1 for aws partition)
- **AWS Account Management** (control plane in us-east-1 for aws partition)
- **Route 53 Application Recovery Controller** (control plane in us-west-2 for aws partition, only exists in aws partition)
- **AWS Network Manager** (control plane in us-west-2 for aws partition)
- **Route 53 Private DNS** (control plane in us-east-1 for aws partition)

The key characteristic: their control plane runs in one Region, but they have isolated data planes distributed across all Regions in the partition.

Take IAM as an example. The IAM control plane in us-east-1 handles all operations that create, modify, or delete IAM resources (users, roles, policies, etc.). However, IAM has isolated data planes in every Region in the aws partition. When your application makes an API call to an AWS service, that call is authenticated and authorized using the IAM data plane in the Region where the service resides—not by reaching back to us-east-1.

**Designing for Static Stability with Partitional Services**: The critical insight is that during a control plane impairment, CRUDL (Create, Read, Update, Delete, List) operations may not work, but authentication and authorization (data plane operations) continue functioning normally.

This means:
- Don't create IAM users or roles during failures—pre-provision "break-glass" users with passwords and access keys already generated
- Don't modify IAM policies during recovery—design policies that work for both normal and failure scenarios
- Don't depend on creating new AWS accounts during disaster recovery—pre-provision all needed accounts
- Don't rely on Organizations control plane for permissions during incidents—use IAM session tags evaluated by data plane

#### 2. Edge Network Services (Global Edge Distribution)

Edge network services have control planes in a single Region but host their data planes in global Points of Presence (PoPs) and potentially AWS Regions:

- **Route 53 Public DNS** (control plane in us-east-1)
- **Amazon CloudFront** (control plane in us-east-1)
- **AWS WAF for CloudFront** (control plane in us-east-1)
- **Amazon Certificate Manager for CloudFront** (control plane in us-east-1)
- **AWS Global Accelerator** (control plane in us-west-2)
- **AWS Shield Advanced** (control plane in us-east-1)

Route 53 exemplifies this pattern. Its control plane in us-east-1 handles creating hosted zones, managing records, and configuring health checks. The data plane runs across hundreds of Points of Presence globally and in each AWS Region, answering DNS queries based on your hosted zone configurations.

Route 53 health checks are performed from eight AWS Regions in the aws partition. This health check data plane operates independently of the control plane, continuing to check endpoint health and update DNS responses even if the control plane is impaired.

**Designing with Edge Network Services**: The key principle is avoiding control plane dependencies in recovery paths:

- Use Route 53 health check status changes to perform failover, not changing record values via control plane APIs
- Pre-provision CloudFront distributions, ELBs, and other resources that create DNS records—don't create them during failures
- Configure CloudFront origin failover beforehand rather than updating distribution configuration during incidents
- Use Route 53 ARC routing controls (data plane) instead of modifying Route 53 records (control plane)

#### 3. Global Single-Region Operations

Some operations within otherwise Regional or zonal services have underlying dependencies on a single Region. These aren't entire services, but specific control plane operations:

**Route 53 Dependencies**: Many AWS services create resources that require DNS records. When you provision an ELB, API Gateway endpoint, or OpenSearch domain, AWS must create Route 53 DNS records. This relies on the Route 53 control plane in us-east-1. Services with this dependency include:

- Amazon API Gateway (REST and HTTP APIs)
- Elastic Load Balancers (all types)
- AWS PrivateLink VPC endpoints
- Lambda function URLs
- Amazon ElastiCache
- Amazon OpenSearch Service
- Amazon MemoryDB
- Amazon Neptune
- DynamoDB Accelerator (DAX)
- AWS Global Accelerator

Note: EC2 instance hostnames in VPC DNS (like `i-0123456789abcdef.ec2.internal`) do NOT depend on Route 53. VPC DNS exists independently in each Region.

**Amazon S3 Global Operations**: Several S3 control plane operations depend on us-east-1:
- Bucket configuration updates (CORS, lifecycle, replication, encryption, logging, etc.)
- CreateBucket/DeleteBucket (to ensure globally unique bucket names)
- S3 Multi-Region Access Points control plane (in us-west-2, with additional dependencies)

**CloudFront for API Gateway**: Edge-optimized API Gateway endpoints depend on the CloudFront control plane in us-east-1 to create distributions.

<Callout type="warning">
Common anti-patterns that introduce global service control plane dependencies:
- Updating Route 53 records to perform failover
- Creating IAM resources during failures (often unintentional in untested failover plans)
- Provisioning disaster recovery resources like ELBs during outages
- Modifying CloudFront distributions to fail away from impaired origins
- Updating Global Accelerator traffic dials for manual failover
</Callout>

## Practical Application: Designing Resilient Architectures

Understanding fault isolation boundaries transforms from academic knowledge to practical skill when you apply it to real architectural decisions. Let's examine specific scenarios.

### Scenario 1: Web Application with Database

You're designing a web application that serves HTTP requests and stores data in a relational database. Users expect 99.95% availability (roughly 4.4 hours downtime per year maximum).

**Architecture Decision Points**:

1. **Compute Layer (Zonal Service - EC2)**:
   - Deploy across three Availability Zones
   - Use Auto Scaling groups, but overprovision by 50% (N+1 pattern)
   - Each zone runs at ~66% capacity during normal operation
   - If one zone fails, two remaining zones provide 133% of required capacity
   - **Avoid**: Relying on Auto Scaling to launch instances during zone failure (control plane dependency)

2. **Load Balancing (Regional Service - ELB)**:
   - Application Load Balancer provides regional endpoint
   - ALB automatically distributes across zones
   - Disable cross-zone load balancing to ensure zone independence
   - Health checks ensure traffic routes only to healthy instances
   - **Avoid**: Needing to create new load balancers during failures

3. **Database (Zonal Service - RDS)**:
   - RDS Multi-AZ deployment (primary in one zone, standby in another)
   - Synchronous replication within Region (zero RPO)
   - Automatic failover when primary zone has issues
   - **Consider**: Read replicas in each zone for read traffic distribution with zone affinity

4. **Global Dependency Management**:
   - Pre-provision all ELBs (they create Route 53 records)
   - Use Route 53 health checks for DNS failover if multi-region
   - Pre-create IAM roles and policies—don't modify during incidents
   - Store RDS endpoint discovery in Parameter Store with caching

This architecture achieves excellent availability within a single Region while minimizing control plane dependencies during failures.

### Scenario 2: Multi-Region Disaster Recovery

Your workload requires cross-Region disaster recovery with RTO of 1 hour and RPO of 5 minutes.

**Architecture Considerations**:

**Primary Region**:
- Full production deployment as described in Scenario 1
- Continuous data replication to DR Region (asynchronous)
- CloudWatch metrics and alarms for failure detection

**DR Region**:
- **Pre-provisioned static resources** (static stability principle):
  - All ELBs already created (avoid Route 53 control plane dependency)
  - IAM roles and policies already exist
  - S3 buckets for application artifacts already created
  - VPC, subnets, security groups pre-configured

- **Scaled-down but ready compute**:
  - Smaller Auto Scaling groups (minimum instances running)
  - Can scale up quickly via data plane operations (still faster than launching from zero)

- **Database**:
  - RDS read replica in DR Region (asynchronous replication from primary)
  - Can be promoted to standalone instance
  - Accept 5-minute RPO from replication lag

**Failover Mechanism**:
- Route 53 health checks monitor primary Region
- When health checks fail, DNS automatically routes to DR Region
- **Avoid**: Manually updating Route 53 records during failover
- **Avoid**: Creating new resources in DR Region during failover
- Application scales up in DR Region using pre-provisioned infrastructure

This design achieves the RTO/RPO targets while minimizing dependencies on global service control planes during the failure scenario that triggers DR.

### Scenario 3: Microservices Platform

You're building a platform serving multiple internal teams, each with their own microservices. You need both high availability and blast radius containment.

**Architecture Approach**:

**Cellular Architecture** (beyond just Availability Zones):
- Divide infrastructure into multiple cells within each Region
- Each cell is a complete, independent copy of the platform
- Route customers/teams to specific cells (using shuffle sharding principles)

**Zone Distribution**:
- Each cell spans three Availability Zones
- Within a cell, implement zone independence
- Across cells, implement cell independence

**Service Mesh**:
- Use service mesh for inter-service communication
- Configure zone-aware routing (prefer same-zone calls)
- Implement circuit breakers at both zone and cell boundaries

**Deployment Practices**:
- Never deploy to multiple cells simultaneously
- Never deploy to multiple zones in a cell simultaneously
- Velocity controls with automated rollback on metrics degradation
- Deployment waves: one zone in one cell, monitor, expand

**Dependencies**:
- Each cell has its own databases, queues, caches
- Shared services (IAM, Route 53) accessed via data plane
- Pre-provisioned capacity in each cell for static stability

This architecture provides multiple layers of isolation: zones protect against infrastructure failures, cells protect against software bugs or tenant-specific issues, and zone-aware routing minimizes cross-zone traffic.

## Services That Use Default Global Endpoints

Some AWS services provide default global endpoints that introduce hidden dependencies on specific Regions. Understanding and remediating these is crucial for resilience.

### AWS STS (Security Token Service)

**Default Behavior**: AWS SDKs and CLI default to the global STS endpoint, which resolves to us-east-1.

**Impact**: If us-east-1 has issues, your application can't request temporary credentials even though you're operating in a different Region.

**Remediation**: Configure SDKs and CLI to use Regional STS endpoints:

```bash
# AWS CLI configuration
aws configure set sts_regional_endpoints regional
```

```python
# Python SDK
import boto3
sts = boto3.client('sts', region_name='eu-west-1')
```

Using SigV4A (signature version 4A) also requires Regional STS endpoints—the global endpoint doesn't support this operation.

### SAML Sign-In

**Default Behavior**: Many IAM SAML configurations use `https://signin.aws.amazon.com/saml` (hosted in us-east-1).

**Impact**: During us-east-1 impairment, federated users can't sign in to AWS even in other Regions.

**Remediation**:
- Update IAM trust policies to accept SAML from multiple Regional endpoints
- Configure IdP to use Regional SAML endpoint: `https://us-west-2.signin.aws.amazon.com/saml`
- During us-east-1 failure, update IdP to use different Regional endpoint
- **Pre-provision break-glass users** in case IdP itself becomes unavailable

### IAM Identity Center

**Default Behavior**: Even when deployed to a specific Region, Identity Center defaults to the global SAML endpoint in us-east-1.

**Impact**: Identity Center deployment in eu-west-1 has hidden dependency on us-east-1.

**Remediation**:
- Update relay state URL of all permission sets to match your Identity Center Region
- Example: If deployed in eu-west-1, use `https://eu-west-1.console.aws.amazon.com`
- Pre-provision break-glass users (Identity Center can only be in one Region)

### S3 Storage Lens

**Default Behavior**: Default dashboard (`default-account-dashboard`) stores configuration and metrics in us-east-1.

**Impact**: Can't access Storage Lens data during us-east-1 impairment.

**Remediation**: Create additional dashboards in other Regions by specifying home Region for dashboard configuration.

## Summary of Recommendations

The fault isolation boundaries AWS provides enable you to build highly available systems, but only if you use them correctly:

### For Zonal Services (EC2, EBS, etc.):
- ✅ Deploy across at least three Availability Zones
- ✅ Implement static stability with pre-provisioned capacity (N+1 or N+2)
- ✅ Disable cross-zone load balancing for true zone independence
- ✅ Use zone-aware service discovery and routing
- ❌ Don't rely on Auto Scaling to launch instances during zone failures
- ❌ Don't use cross-zone dependencies in critical paths

### For Regional Services (DynamoDB, SQS, etc.):
- ✅ Trust the service to handle zone failures internally
- ✅ Use the Regional endpoint without specifying zones
- ✅ For extra resilience, deploy across multiple Regions
- ❌ Don't try to manually manage zone distribution
- ❌ Don't assume synchronous cross-Region replication is available

### For Global Services:
- ✅ Pre-provision all resources to avoid control plane dependencies during failures
- ✅ Use data plane operations (Route 53 health checks) for failover
- ✅ Configure Regional STS endpoints instead of global default
- ✅ Update SAML and Identity Center to use Regional endpoints
- ✅ Create break-glass IAM users with pre-generated credentials
- ❌ Don't create/modify IAM resources during incidents
- ❌ Don't update Route 53 records for failover
- ❌ Don't create S3 buckets or update bucket configurations during recovery
- ❌ Don't provision new ELBs, API Gateways, or other resources that create DNS records during failures

## How This Applies

When you're designing a new system or reviewing an existing one, ask yourself:

1. **What are my fault isolation boundaries?** Identify whether you're using zonal, regional, or global services, and understand the failure domains.

2. **Where are my control plane dependencies?** Map out which control plane operations your recovery procedures require. Can you eliminate them through pre-provisioning?

3. **Is my system statically stable?** If an Availability Zone fails, can your system continue operating with existing resources, or does it need to create new ones?

4. **What global services do I depend on?** For each global service, identify the control plane location. Have you designed your recovery procedures to use only data plane operations?

5. **Have I tested my assumptions?** Run game days that simulate control plane impairments. Do your failover procedures work when you can't create resources?

By understanding fault isolation boundaries and designing systems that respect these boundaries, you transform the theoretical availability of AWS infrastructure into practical, measurable availability for your workloads.

<KeyTakeaways>
- **Hierarchical isolation boundaries**: AWS provides Availability Zones (independent infrastructure), Regions (geographic isolation), and Partitions (administrative boundaries), each serving specific fault isolation purposes
- **Control planes handle changes, data planes handle work**: Control planes are more complex and statistically more likely to fail; data planes are simpler and more reliable—design for static stability by avoiding control plane dependencies during failures
- **Zonal services provide maximum isolation**: EC2, EBS, and other zonal services let you distribute across independent zones; implement N+1 capacity to survive zone failures without launching new resources
- **Regional services abstract complexity**: Services like DynamoDB and SQS handle multi-AZ distribution internally; trust them for single-Region resilience but understand they're not substitutes for multi-Region DR when needed
- **Global services require careful design**: Partitional services (IAM, Organizations), edge network services (Route 53, CloudFront), and global operations (S3 bucket creation, ELB provisioning) have control planes in specific Regions—pre-provision resources and use data plane operations for resilient failover
- **Static stability principle**: The most reliable recovery mechanisms require no changes; pre-provision capacity, resources, and configurations rather than depending on control plane operations during failures
</KeyTakeaways>
