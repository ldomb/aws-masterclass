## Introduction

Edward Hopper's "Nighthawks" is one of the most iconic American paintings of the 20th century. It depicts a late-night diner, bathed in fluorescent light, where three patrons sit with their coffee while a white-coated server works behind the counter. But if you look carefully at the painting, you'll notice two massive coffee urns looming behind the server—each as large as a trash can, capable of brewing hundreds of cups of coffee at once.

These coffee urns aren't just aesthetic details. They represent an elegant engineering solution to a fundamental scalability problem: how do you serve coffee to an unpredictable number of customers with consistent quality and minimal wait time? The answer is brilliant in its simplicity: the urns keep many cups of coffee warm all the time, regardless of whether three late-night diners or three hundred morning commuters want a cup.

This is the essence of **constant work**—performing the same amount of work regardless of demand. In computing terminology, coffee urns are O(1), not O(N). They have no scaling factor. Whether three people or three hundred people show up, the urns do exactly the same thing: maintain temperature for a fixed amount of coffee.

What makes coffee urns even more remarkable is their anti-fragility. As more coffee is drawn out during busy periods, there's actually less coffee left to keep warm, making the urns more efficient precisely when they're under the most stress. They perform better when you need them most.

This lesson explores how AWS applies this constant work principle to build some of its most critical and reliable systems. You'll learn how Amazon Route 53 health checks and AWS Hyperplane network load balancer configuration use constant work patterns to achieve extreme reliability. More importantly, you'll understand when constant work is the right design choice and when it's not—because like all patterns, it has its place and its limitations.

## The Variability Challenge in Distributed Systems

One of the great advantages of computers over humans is their perfect repeatability. A skilled barista might craft an excellent cup of coffee, but they'll never make it exactly the same way twice. Small variations in temperature, grind, timing, and technique accumulate. More importantly, scaling human processes is hard—one barista making 100 cups will create long lines of waiting customers.

Computers are different. Teach a computer how to perform a task once, and it will execute that task millions of times identically. The quality and craft go into how you program it, but once programmed, execution is flawless every single time.

However, there's a catch: doing something millions of times takes longer than doing it thousands of times, which takes longer than doing it once. When you ask a computer to add two plus two a million times, it will get four every single time, but it will take longer than if you only asked once.

This seems obvious, but it has profound implications for building highly reliable systems. When operating at scale, **variability is your biggest enemy**. This is never more true than when handling:

- **Increases in load**: When traffic suddenly spikes, systems must respond without slowdowns
- **State changes like reconfigurations**: When configuration changes propagate, they must do so predictably
- **Responses to failures**: When power is lost or networks partition, recovery must be immediate

Times of high stress on a system—moments when many changes are happening simultaneously—are precisely the worst times for things to get slower. Yet without careful design, that's exactly what happens.

When systems slow down, queues get longer. Just like lines forming at a barista-powered café, request queues build up in distributed systems. But unlike a café queue where customers might wait patiently or leave to find another coffee shop, system queues can trigger what's known as a **spiral of doom** or cascading failure.

Here's how it unfolds: As the system gets slower, clients detect timeouts and retry their requests (a reasonable defensive strategy). But those retries add more load to the already struggling system, making it even slower. This triggers even more timeouts and even more retries. The system feeds on itself in a vicious cycle until it collapses entirely.

<Callout type="warning">
Even with perfect timeout and retry logic—following all the best practices for exponential backoff with jitter—slowdowns during failures are still deeply problematic. Delay in responding to failures means downtime. The goal isn't just to handle failures gracefully; it's to eliminate variability in how quickly systems respond to failures.
</Callout>

This is why many of AWS's most reliable systems use very simple, very dumb, very reliable **constant work patterns**—just like coffee urns. These patterns have three essential characteristics:

1. **They don't scale up or slow down with load or stress**: Performance remains consistent whether serving one request or one million
2. **They don't have modes**: The system performs the same operations in all conditions—no special "failure mode" or "recovery mode"
3. **If they vary at all, they do less work in times of stress**: Anti-fragile behavior that makes them more efficient when you need reliability most

As Colm MacCárthaigh writes: "These patterns have three key features. One, they don't scale up or slow down with load or stress. Two, they don't have modes, which means they do the same operations in all conditions. Three, if they have any variation, it's to do less work in times of stress so they can perform better when you need them most. There's that anti-fragility again."

## Caches and Anti-Fragility: A Cautionary Tale

Whenever the topic of anti-fragility comes up, someone inevitably mentions caches as the perfect example. After all, caches improve response times, and they tend to improve those response times even better under load. As more requests come in, cache hit rates increase, reducing load on backend systems. More traffic leads to better performance—textbook anti-fragility, right?

Not quite.

Most caches have **modes**, which fundamentally contradicts the constant work principle. When a cache is empty (called a "cold cache"), response times become dramatically worse. All requests must hit the slower backend system. The system becomes unstable as the backend—which was sized based on the assumption that most requests would be served from cache—suddenly receives full load.

The problems compound. When a cache is rendered ineffective by changing access patterns, key evictions, or simply too much load with poor locality, it can trigger a **cascading failure** where the source system falls over from direct load. If your backend database was scaled to handle 10% of requests (because 90% hit the cache), and your cache suddenly goes cold, your database now receives 10× its design capacity.

Caches appear anti-fragile at first glance, but they actually **amplify fragility** when over-stressed or invalidated. They create bimodal behavior: extraordinarily fast when warm, painfully slow when cold. This mode-based behavior—"cache mode" versus "no-cache mode"—directly violates the second principle of constant work patterns.

This doesn't mean caches are bad. It means you must build them carefully, understanding their failure modes. Matt Brinkley and Jas Chhabra have written extensively in the Amazon Builders' Library about what it takes to build a truly anti-fragile cache. The key insight is that avoiding modes requires either keeping caches always warm or ensuring your backend can handle full load, treating the cache as a performance optimization rather than a critical dependency.

## Amazon Route 53 Health Checks: Constant Work in Action

It's hard to think of a more critical function in distributed systems than health checks. When an instance, server, or entire Availability Zone loses power or network connectivity, health checks must detect the failure immediately and redirect traffic elsewhere. Every second of delay means customer requests failing.

Health checks are integrated throughout AWS—in Elastic Load Balancing load balancers, Auto Scaling groups, and many other services. But the most critical health checks of all are those in Amazon Route 53, the DNS service. If DNS isn't sending traffic to healthy endpoints, there's no other opportunity to recover. Route 53 health checks are the last line of defense.

### How Route 53 Health Checks Work

From a customer's perspective, Route 53 health checks work through a simple association: a DNS name maps to two or more answers (typically IP addresses for service endpoints). These answers might be weighted to distribute traffic proportionally, or configured in a primary-secondary pattern where one answer takes precedence as long as it remains healthy.

The health of each endpoint is determined by associating each potential answer with a health check. Customers create health checks by configuring:

- A target (usually the same IP address that's in the DNS answer)
- A port and protocol (HTTP, HTTPS, TCP)
- Request parameters and expected responses
- Timeout values and check intervals
- Failure threshold (how many failed checks before marking unhealthy)

If you use Elastic Load Balancing, Amazon RDS, or many other AWS services that leverage Route 53 for high availability and failover, those services configure all of this automatically on your behalf.

Behind the scenes, Route 53 operates a **fleet of health checkers** that are broadly distributed across many AWS Regions with substantial redundancy. Every few seconds, tens of health checkers send requests to each target and verify the results. These health check results are then sent to a smaller **fleet of aggregators**.

The aggregators apply intelligent logic about health check sensitivity. Just because one of ten health checks in the latest round failed doesn't necessarily mean the target is unhealthy. Health checks can be subject to noise—transient network glitches, brief CPU spikes, or momentary overload. The aggregators apply conditioning logic: for example, a target might only be considered unhealthy if at least three individual health checks have failed.

Customers can configure these sensitivity options, and the aggregators apply whatever logic each customer has defined for their targets.

### Constant Work at Every Layer

So far, everything described lends itself perfectly to constant work. It doesn't matter whether targets are healthy or unhealthy—the health checkers and aggregators do exactly the same work every time. Ten health checkers send requests to each target. The aggregators receive results, apply their conditioning logic, and output a health status.

New customers might configure additional health checks against new targets, and each one adds slightly to the overall workload. But AWS doesn't need to worry about this growth for two key reasons.

First, Route 53's health checkers and aggregators use a **cellular design**. AWS has tested how many health checks each cell can sustain, and always knows where each cell stands relative to that tested limit. If the system starts approaching those limits, AWS adds another health checking cell or aggregator cell, whichever is needed. The system scales horizontally by adding cells, not by pushing individual cells beyond their limits.

Second—and this is perhaps the cleverest trick in this entire article—even when there are only a few health checks active, the health checkers send result sets sized to their maximum capacity. For example, if only 10 health checks are configured on a particular health checker, but that checker could ultimately support 10,000 health checks, it still constantly sends out a set of 10,000 results. The other 9,990 entries are dummies—placeholder data that has no meaning but fills the space.

Why does this matter? Because it ensures that the network load, as well as the computational work the aggregators are performing, won't increase as customers configure more health checks. A massive source of variance has been eliminated.

What's most important for reliability is what happens during failures. Imagine a scenario where a very large number of targets start failing their health checks all at once—perhaps an entire Availability Zone loses power, affecting thousands of instances. In a traditional system, this would trigger a flurry of activity: detecting failures, propagating state changes, updating configurations, and redirecting traffic.

But in Route 53's constant work design, **it makes absolutely no difference** to the health checkers or aggregators how many targets fail. They continue doing what they were already doing. The same number of health checks are performed. The same size result sets are transmitted. The same aggregation logic is applied. The only thing that changes is the content of the results—more "unhealthy" statuses instead of "healthy" statuses.

In fact, the overall system might actually do slightly less work during an Availability Zone failure. That's because some of the redundant health checkers might themselves be located in the impacted Availability Zone, so there are fewer health checkers performing checks. This is anti-fragility in action.

### The Critical Decision: DNS Query Processing

Route 53 could have taken a very different approach. It would be natural to think of health check results as triggers for DNS changes. Compare the latest health check status to the previous one. If a status changes to unhealthy, create an API request to remove any associated answers from DNS. If a status changes back to healthy, create another API request to add it back. Or perhaps implement an "is_active" flag that could be toggled on demand.

If you think of Route 53 as a sort of database with records to update, this event-driven approach seems to make sense. But it would be a critical mistake.

First, a single health check might be associated with many DNS answers. The same IP address might appear many times across different DNS names. When a health check fails, making these changes might mean updating one record, or it might mean updating hundreds.

Next, consider the unlikely but possible event of an Availability Zone losing power. Tens of thousands of health checks might start failing simultaneously. There could be millions of DNS changes to make. Processing all those changes would take considerable time—and that's not an acceptable way to respond to a power outage. Customers need their traffic redirected in seconds, not minutes.

**Route 53's design is fundamentally different.** Every few seconds, the health check aggregators send a **fixed-size table** of health check statuses to all the Route 53 DNS servers. When the DNS servers receive this table, they store it in memory, essentially as-is. That's constant work in its purest form: every few seconds, receive a table, store it in memory. No conditional logic. No "if this status changed, then update that record." Just receive, store, done.

Why does Route 53 push the data to DNS servers rather than having DNS servers pull from the aggregators? That's because there are more DNS servers than there are health check aggregators. As Joe Magerramov explains in his article on putting the smaller service in control, having the smaller fleet (aggregators) push to the larger fleet (DNS servers) prevents the larger fleet from overwhelming the smaller one with pull requests.

When a Route 53 DNS server receives a DNS query, it performs a simple sequence of operations:

1. Look up all potential answers for the queried DNS name
2. Cross-reference each answer with its associated health check status from the in-memory table
3. Filter out any answers whose health check status is unhealthy
4. Select from the remaining healthy answers (based on weights, geolocation, latency, or other routing policies)
5. Return the selected answer(s)

Critically, even if the first answer checked is healthy and eligible, **the server still checks the other potential answers anyway**. This ensures that even if health check statuses change mid-query, the DNS server is still performing the same work it was before. There's no increase in scan time or retrieval time based on how many answers are healthy versus unhealthy.

<Callout type="info">
The DNS servers simply don't care how many health checks are healthy, how many are unhealthy, or how many suddenly change status. The code performs the exact same actions every single time. There's no new mode of operation. AWS didn't make a large set of changes, nor did it pull a lever that activated some kind of "Availability Zone unreachable" mode. The only difference is which answers Route 53 chooses as results. The same memory is accessed. The same amount of computing time is spent.
</Callout>

This makes the process extraordinarily reliable precisely during the most critical moments—when failures are actually occurring and traffic needs to be redirected immediately.

## AWS Hyperplane Configuration: Amazon S3 as a Configuration Loop

Another application that demands extreme reliability is the configuration of foundational network components like Network Load Balancers. When a customer adds a new EC2 instance or container as a target to their load balancer, it's often both critical and urgent. Perhaps they're experiencing a flash crowd—a sudden spike in traffic—and need to add capacity immediately to avoid overload.

Under the hood, Network Load Balancers run on AWS Hyperplane, an internal network function virtualization service that's embedded deep in the Amazon EC2 network infrastructure. Hyperplane processes packets, applies routing rules, performs health checks, and distributes traffic across targets.

### The Workflow Approach (Not Used)

AWS Hyperplane could handle configuration changes through a traditional workflow system. The sequence would look like this:

1. Customer makes a change to their Network Load Balancer (adds a target, changes health check settings, etc.)
2. The change is turned into an event
3. The event is inserted into a workflow system
4. The workflow pushes that change out to all the AWS Hyperplane nodes that need it
5. Each node ingests the change and updates its configuration

This is a reasonable approach that many systems use. But there's a fundamental problem: **when there are a large number of changes happening all at once, the system will very likely slow down**. More changes mean more work. More workflow events to process. More state to track. More complex coordination.

When systems slow down, customers naturally resort to trying again. Maybe the change didn't seem to take effect immediately, so they retry. Or their automation detects a failure and automatically retries. These retries add even more load to the already struggling workflow system, making it slow down even further.

This is exactly the kind of variability and potential spiral of doom that constant work patterns are designed to avoid.

### The Constant Work Solution

The solution AWS Hyperplane uses is surprisingly simple and elegant:

1. When a customer makes a change, AWS Hyperplane immediately integrates that change into a **configuration file stored in Amazon S3**
2. AWS Hyperplane nodes **fetch this configuration from Amazon S3 every few seconds**
3. The nodes **process and load this configuration file**, even if nothing has changed
4. This happens continuously, regardless of whether the configuration is completely identical to the last time or completely different

The system is effectively **always processing the maximum number of configuration changes**. Whether one load balancer changed, ten changed, or hundreds changed, the AWS Hyperplane nodes behave exactly the same way: fetch the configuration, process it, load it, repeat.

You can probably see this coming now, but the configuration file is also **sized to its maximum size right from the beginning**. Even when AWS activates a new Region and there are only a handful of Network Load Balancers active, the configuration file is still as large as it will ever be. There are dummy configuration "slots" waiting to be filled with actual customer configuration. But as far as the internal workings of AWS Hyperplane are concerned, those slots are there nonetheless.

The nodes process the same size file, allocate the same amount of memory, perform the same parsing operations, and execute the same application logic whether there are 10 load balancers configured or 10,000. Variance has been eliminated.

### Anti-Fragility and Cost-Effectiveness

Because AWS Hyperplane is a highly redundant system distributed across many physical hosts, there's genuine anti-fragility in this design. If AWS Hyperplane nodes are lost—perhaps due to hardware failure or network partitioning—the amount of work in the system goes down, not up. There are fewer nodes making requests to Amazon S3, instead of more frantic attempts in a workflow system trying to reach nodes that are no longer available.

Besides being simple and robust, this approach is remarkably **cost-effective**. Storing a file in Amazon S3 and fetching it over and over again in a loop, even from hundreds or thousands of machines, costs far less than the engineering time and opportunity cost that would be spent building, testing, maintaining, and operating something more complex.

The S3 GET requests are inexpensive. The bandwidth is minimal (configuration files are relatively small). The compute cost of processing the configuration is negligible compared to the packet processing the nodes are already doing. And the operational simplicity means engineers can focus on improving the data plane functionality rather than debugging complex workflow systems.

## Constant Work and Self-Healing

There's another fascinating property of constant work designs that hasn't been mentioned yet: **they tend to be naturally self-healing** and automatically correct a wide variety of problems without any human intervention.

Consider what happens if a configuration file is somehow corrupted while being applied. Perhaps it was mistakenly truncated by a transient network problem during download. In a traditional event-driven system, this might require complex error detection, retry logic, and potentially manual intervention to repair the corrupted state.

In a constant work system, **the problem automatically corrects itself on the next cycle**. A few seconds later, the node fetches the configuration again, gets a clean copy, and applies it successfully. No special error handling needed. No alerts to oncall engineers. No manual recovery procedures. The corruption is healed automatically.

Or say a DNS server missed a health check status update entirely—perhaps it was in the middle of a deployment restart when the update arrived. Again, no problem. It will receive the next update without building up any kind of backlog or getting permanently out of sync. The next update isn't a delta or increment that depends on receiving the previous update; it's a complete, self-contained snapshot of the current state.

Since a constant work system is continuously starting from a clean slate, it's always operating in what you might call **"repair everything" mode**. There's no separate recovery logic or special repair procedures. The normal operation is the repair operation.

<Callout type="tip">
The self-healing property of constant work is particularly valuable during incidents. When systems are under stress and multiple things are going wrong simultaneously, having a design that continuously repairs itself without human intervention or complex recovery procedures dramatically improves mean time to recovery (MTTR). Engineers can focus on addressing root causes rather than manually repairing state.
</Callout>

In contrast, workflow-based systems are usually **edge-triggered**, meaning that changes in configuration or state are what kick off the occurrence of workflow actions. These changes first have to be correctly detected, and then actions often have to occur in a perfect sequence to work properly. The system needs complex logic to handle cases where some actions don't succeed, need to be rolled back, or need to be repaired because of transient corruption.

Edge-triggered workflow systems are also prone to the buildup of backlogs. If the system can't keep up with the rate of change events, the backlog grows. If the backlog grows large enough, it can become difficult or impossible to ever catch up.

Workflows aren't naturally self-healing—you have to deliberately make them self-healing through additional complexity, careful design, and thorough testing. Constant work systems get self-healing essentially for free as an emergent property of their design.

## Design and Manageability: Understanding O(1)

Earlier in this article, big-O notation was mentioned—describing constant work systems as O(1). It's important to understand what this actually means, because there's a common misconception.

O(1) doesn't mean that a process or algorithm uses only one operation. It means that it uses a **constant number of operations regardless of the size of the input**. The notation should really be O(C), where C is some constant.

Both the Network Load Balancer configuration system and the Route 53 health check system are actually performing many thousands of operations for every "tick" or "cycle" that they iterate through. Route 53 DNS servers are performing lookups, cross-references, filtering, and selection. AWS Hyperplane nodes are parsing configuration files, validating entries, updating routing tables, and more.

But those operations don't change in number or complexity because health check statuses changed, or because customer configurations were added or modified. **That's the entire point.** The amount of work is constant with respect to the changes in the system.

They're like coffee urns that hold hundreds of cups of coffee at a time, regardless of how many customers are currently looking for a cup.

### When Constant Work Doesn't Fit

In the physical world, constant work patterns usually come at the cost of waste. If you brew a whole coffee urn but only get a handful of coffee drinkers during your shift, you're going to end up pouring most of that coffee down the drain at the end of the day. You've lost the energy it took to heat the coffee urn, the energy it took to sanitize and transport the water, and the coffee grounds themselves.

For a café or caterer, these costs turn out to be relatively small and very acceptable. The cost of wasted coffee is far less than the cost of long lines of angry customers or the complexity of trying to brew individual cups on demand. There may even be more waste brewing one cup at a time, because some economies of scale are lost—you can't efficiently brew half a pot.

For configuration systems or health check propagation systems, this waste issue essentially doesn't arise. The difference in actual energy cost between propagating one health check result and propagating 10,000 health check results is negligible. Computers are already running, consuming power, and doing work. The marginal cost of processing a larger data structure or sending a larger network packet is minimal.

In fact, because constant work patterns don't need separate retry mechanisms, backoff logic, and complex state machines to handle failures and edge cases, they can actually **save energy and computational resources** compared to a design that uses an event-driven workflow.

However, there are definitely cases where the constant work pattern doesn't fit well, and it's important to recognize them.

If you're running a large website that requires 100 web servers at peak traffic times, you could choose to always run 100 web servers around the clock. This would certainly reduce a significant source of variance in the system—you'd never experience the dynamism and potential issues associated with scaling up and down. It follows the spirit of constant work design.

But it's also wasteful in a way that matters. For web servers, scaling elastically can be a much better fit because the savings are substantial. It's not unusual for a website to require half as many web servers during off-peak hours as during peak hours. Over the course of a year, that's a tremendous amount of computing capacity that would be wasted.

Because this daily scaling happens every single day, the system experiences the dynamism regularly and frequently enough to shake out problems. Issues with scaling up or down are discovered and fixed quickly because they happen all the time. The savings from elastic scaling can be enjoyed by both the customer and the planet (through reduced energy consumption).

<Callout type="warning">
**When to use constant work**: Constant work is not universally applicable. Use it for systems where:
- Work variance introduces significant reliability risk during failures
- The cost of constant work is negligible compared to event-driven alternatives
- Self-healing and utterly predictable behavior are critical requirements
- The system processes configuration or state propagation rather than customer requests
- The system is part of critical infrastructure where reliability trumps efficiency
</Callout>

The key is understanding the trade-offs. Constant work sacrifices some efficiency for dramatically improved reliability and simplicity. For critical infrastructure components—DNS, load balancer configuration, network routing—that trade-off is worthwhile. For customer-facing compute resources that directly scale with workload, elastic scaling is typically more appropriate.

## The Value of Simple Design

The word "simple" has been used several times throughout this article to describe constant work patterns. But it's worth being precise about what "simple" actually means in this context, because counting moving parts can be deceptive.

A unicycle has fewer moving parts than a bicycle, but it's dramatically harder to ride. That's not simpler in any meaningful sense. A good design has to handle many different stresses, failures, and edge cases, and over enough time, "survival of the fittest" tends to eliminate designs that have either too many or too few moving parts, or that aren't practical to operate.

When we say a **simple design**, we mean a design that is:

- **Easy to understand**: New team members can grasp how it works without weeks of study
- **Easy to use**: Interacting with the system doesn't require complex procedures or arcane knowledge
- **Easy to operate**: Running the system in production doesn't require constant manual intervention

If a design makes intuitive sense to a team that had absolutely nothing to do with its original inception, that's a very good sign. At AWS, the constant work design pattern has been successfully reused many times across many different services and teams. You might be surprised by just how many configuration systems can be as elegantly simple as "fetch and apply a full configuration each time in a loop."

The pattern survives and thrives because it genuinely is simple in all three dimensions:

**Easy to understand**: There's no complex state machine to reason about. No subtle edge cases where behavior differs. The system does the same thing every time. You can explain it in a paragraph.

**Easy to use**: From a customer perspective, changes take effect within a predictable time window (the polling interval). There's no distinction between "fast updates" and "slow updates" or between "easy changes" and "hard changes." All changes work the same way.

**Easy to operate**: When something goes wrong, there are no special recovery procedures. No manual state repairs. No complex runbooks. The system automatically heals itself on the next cycle. Operators can focus on addressing root causes rather than manually fixing symptoms.

These characteristics make constant work patterns ideal for the most critical infrastructure components, where reliability trumps all other considerations. The pattern has survived at Amazon precisely because of its operational simplicity and battle-tested reliability.

## Conclusion

Coffee urns in a late-night diner might seem worlds apart from AWS's Route 53 health checking infrastructure or Hyperplane network routing configuration. But they share a fundamental design principle: perform constant work regardless of demand, eliminating variability precisely when you need reliability most.

This lesson has explored how AWS applies constant work patterns to critical systems:

- **Route 53 health checks** use fixed-size payloads and in-memory tables to ensure that checking and propagating health status performs identical work whether one target fails or thousands fail simultaneously
- **AWS Hyperplane** configuration uses Amazon S3 as a constant polling target, fetching and applying configuration every few seconds regardless of whether anything changed
- Both systems exhibit **natural self-healing**, automatically correcting corruption and missed updates without special recovery logic
- The **operational simplicity** of constant work makes these systems easier to understand, test, and operate than more complex event-driven alternatives

The constant work pattern isn't universally applicable. It makes trade-offs, sacrificing some efficiency for dramatically improved reliability and predictability. For critical infrastructure, that trade-off is worthwhile. For elastic compute resources that directly scale with workload, other patterns are more appropriate.

The key insight is recognizing when variability itself is the enemy—when the unpredictability of event-driven systems, the complexity of workflow state machines, and the fragility of edge-triggered logic introduce more risk than they solve. In those cases, the humble coffee urn shows us the way: do the same work all the time, and be reliable.

<KeyTakeaways>
- **Constant work eliminates variability during stress**: Systems performing identical operations regardless of load, state changes, or failures avoid slowdowns precisely when reliability matters most, preventing spirals of doom during cascading failures
- **Fixed-size payloads prevent scaling work with demand**: Route 53 sending 10,000-entry result sets even with only 10 health checks ensures network load and processing work remain constant as customer usage grows, eliminating a major source of variance
- **Polling can be more reliable than event-driven workflows**: AWS Hyperplane nodes fetching configuration from S3 every few seconds provides natural self-healing and utterly predictable performance compared to complex workflow-based change propagation systems
- **Self-healing is an emergent property of constant work**: Systems starting from a clean slate every cycle automatically correct corrupted state, missed updates, and transient errors without special recovery logic—they're always in "repair everything" mode
- **Simple doesn't mean fewer parts—it means easy to understand, use, and operate**: The constant work pattern survives because it's intuitive to reason about, straightforward to test, and requires minimal operational procedures, making it ideal for critical infrastructure
- **Constant work trades efficiency for reliability in critical systems**: While the pattern may waste some resources by processing unchanged data, this cost is negligible for infrastructure components and dramatically improves reliability compared to event-driven alternatives
</KeyTakeaways>
