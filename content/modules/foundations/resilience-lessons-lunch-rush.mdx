## Introduction

Years before joining Amazon, Mike Haken worked in restaurants — as a server, line cook, bartender, and delivery driver. He noticed that most places dealt with unpredictable demand using the same handful of strategies: managing queues, prioritizing critical work, and employing specific tactics to keep service quality predictable even under stress.

When Haken started working at Amazon, he was struck by how closely the approaches to handling load in restaurants resembled the strategies AWS uses for distributed systems. The parallels are remarkable. A restaurant has fundamentally **static capacity** — fixed tables, fixed cooks. Cloud services are **elastic** and can rapidly scale, but the underlying behaviors when load exceeds capacity are surprisingly similar.

This lesson is a **tour of the patterns** Amazon uses to control excessive load, framed through restaurant analogies. Each pattern is introduced here at a conceptual level. Most of them have a full dedicated lesson elsewhere in the course — those deep dives are linked inline. Read this lesson to build a mental map; read the dedicated lessons to learn how to apply each pattern in practice.

<Callout type="info">
At Amazon, true overload is rare because of forecasting, auto-scaling, and pre-provisioned capacity buffers. The strategies in this lesson are safety mechanisms for exceptional events, not the primary operating mode.
</Callout>

The patterns fall into three groups:

- **Well-protected services**: architectural and operational practices that prevent services from being overwhelmed
- **Well-behaved clients**: patterns that prevent clients from overwhelming their dependencies during stress
- **Observability**: detection mechanisms that rapidly identify overload

## Operational practice: Load shedding

In a restaurant, when staff get busy they deprioritize "side work" — refilling salsa, rolling napkins — and focus on the most important customer-facing tasks. The kitchen and dining room stay caught up by intentionally dropping non-essential work.

In distributed systems, this is **load shedding**: cheaply rejecting work instead of spending limited resources on it. When offered load passes a tipping point, accepting more work actually *lowers* the system's **goodput** (work it can successfully complete within client timeouts). Load shedding keeps goodput flat at the maximum sustainable level, protecting the requests the service does accept.

Load shedding is indiscriminate by nature — it sheds whatever requests happen to arrive at the moment, regardless of which customer sent them. That makes it a coarse tool, used in coordination with the fairness patterns below.

## Operational practice: Fairness and quotas

Some restaurants give parties a time limit on holidays so one party can't monopolize a table all night. Limiting any single party's resource use lets the restaurant serve more customers fairly.

AWS does the equivalent with **per-customer quotas** so that one customer driving heavy load can't degrade service for everyone else — a "single-tenant experience in a multi-tenant system." Quotas are enforced through rate-limiting algorithms (token bucket, leaky bucket, sliding window) and exposed via published service quotas with CloudWatch usage metrics.

The cost: occasionally throttling a customer who's exceeding their share, even if total system capacity is available. Worth it to keep behavior predictable.

→ Full treatment of fairness, quotas, and rate-limiting algorithms in [Preventing Noisy Neighbors in Shared Systems](/course/service-dependencies/fairness-multi-tenant).

## Operational practice: Auto Scaling

Restaurants forecast: as seasons change, they stock different ingredients. But forecasts don't handle surprise spikes — if National Gazpacho Day spikes orders in December, the restaurant either improvises or runs out.

AWS uses **automated capacity forecasting** combined with EC2 Auto Scaling to keep capacity ahead of demand. Substantial buffers handle large fluctuations. For unforecasted spikes faster than auto-scaling can react, load shedding and rate limiting protect the service while new capacity comes online.

→ Auto-scaling and capacity management is covered as part of the broader availability discussion in [Measuring and Designing for High Availability](/course/foundations/availability-and-beyond).

## Architectural practice: Managing queue depth

When a restaurant's wait list grows too long, wait-time estimates become inaccurate, customers leave, and the kitchen risks preparing meals for tables that have already walked out. Servers and kitchen need to communicate to drop work for tables that have given up.

Cloud systems face the same problem with deep FIFO queues: workers spend time on messages the client has already timed out on. The oldest messages in deep queues are the most likely to be wasted work, yet FIFO processes them first. The remedies — bounded queues, LIFO, time-based discard, priority queues — all rest on one principle: **it's better to explicitly reject work than to accept it and fail to complete it.**

A related danger is **metastable failure**: a degraded state that persists long after the original trigger is gone, because the system keeps doing wasteful work on the backlog.

## Architectural practice: Constant work

At private events, restaurants don't take individual orders — they send out platters of hors d'oeuvres at fixed intervals. The kitchen does the same amount of work regardless of how many guests are present. Some food may be wasted, but service is predictable and the kitchen never gets blown up.

AWS uses the same **constant work** pattern in critical infrastructure: instead of pushing configuration on change, servers poll an S3 bucket every few seconds and re-apply the configuration *even if nothing changed*. This eliminates variability precisely when reliability matters most — during failures or load spikes, the system does exactly the same work as during normal operation.

→ Full treatment, including Route 53 health-check fixed-size payloads and AWS Hyperplane's S3-based config loop, in [The Constant Work Pattern](/course/foundations/reliability-constant-work).

## Architectural practice: Putting the smaller service in control

Bartenders on a busy night don't let customers shout drink orders at them — they walk the bar and ask each customer in turn. The smaller fleet (bartenders) controls the rate at which the larger fleet (customers) is served.

The same applies to control planes (small) talking to data planes (large). When the data plane outnumbers the control plane 100:1 or more, letting the larger fleet poll the smaller fleet creates overload risk: any synchronized event (reboot, retry storm, bug) can drive concurrent requests well past the control plane's capacity. AWS prefers patterns where the small fleet dictates the pace — via S3 intermediaries, reverse connections, or UDP notifications for extreme scale mismatches.

→ Full treatment, including AWS Hyperplane and the UDP notification pattern, in [Inverting the Flow: Letting Small Services Lead](/course/traffic-management/smaller-service-in-control).

## Well-behaved clients: Circuit breakers

When the kitchen got overwhelmed, wait staff sometimes paused new order acceptance entirely so cooks could clear the queue. But pausing all orders when only the grill is backed up — and the salad station is fine — unnecessarily turned a small problem into a large one.

**Circuit breakers** in software are the same pattern: when a dependency is failing, the client stops sending requests for a backoff period. Done well (with granular fault domains aligned to the dependency's actual failure boundaries), they prevent the client from compounding the dependency's problem. Done poorly (whole-dependency breakers), they amplify a small partition-level issue into a complete outage.

→ Full treatment of bulkheads, per-API isolation, and partition-aware circuit breakers in [Bulkheads and Concurrency Limits](/course/service-dependencies/dependency-isolation). For why Amazon often prefers *not* to use circuit breakers at all, see [Why Fallback Is a Trap](/course/traffic-management/avoiding-fallback).

## Well-behaved clients: Adaptive retries

A patient customer asks their server for salad dressing once and waits. An impatient customer flags down every server who walks by — and ends up with five extra sides of dressing while their actual order is delayed.

Naive retries do the same to dependencies: each retry adds load to a system that's already struggling, and retry amplification through deep call chains can multiply load by k^n. **Adaptive retries** add two protections: a retry quota (token bucket) that limits retries when error rates are high, and client-side rate limiting that throttles new requests when the dependency signals overload.

→ Full treatment of exponential backoff, jitter, retry budgets, and the eight failure modes that make retries necessary in [Retry Strategies That Don't Make Things Worse](/course/traffic-management/timeouts-retries-backoff).

## Observability

Restaurants have a front-of-house manager and a back-of-house manager because each sees a different part of the operation. If you only watch the kitchen, you miss the line forming at the door.

Operating resilient distributed systems requires the same diverse perspectives — client-side, server-side, dependency-side, and per-fault-domain dimensions (per-API, per-AZ, per-customer). Aggregate metrics hide problems; dimensioned metrics surface them.

→ Full treatment of instrumentation, dimensions, and structured logging in [End-to-End Instrumentation Strategies](/course/observability/instrumenting-distributed-systems) and dashboard design in [Dashboards for Rapid Incident Response](/course/observability/dashboards-operational-visibility).

## Conclusion

Amazon's primary strategy for managing load is proactive — forecasting and scaling to stay well ahead of demand. The patterns in this lesson are what kicks in when reality outruns the forecast.

Each pattern has targeted use cases and trade-offs. Used together, they let AWS deliver consistent customer experience under conditions where naive systems would collapse:

- **Operational practices** (load shedding, fairness, auto-scaling) are reactive defenses when load exceeds expectations.
- **Architectural practices** (constant work, queue management, smaller-service-in-control) build resilience into the design from the start.
- **Client-side practices** (circuit breakers, adaptive retries) ensure clients don't amplify problems when dependencies struggle.

Like a well-run restaurant during the lunch rush, the goal isn't to avoid every difficult moment — it's to handle each one with predictable, practiced responses.

<KeyTakeaways>
- **Three categories of resilience patterns**: well-protected services (operational + architectural), well-behaved clients, and observability — they work together, not in isolation
- **Load shedding keeps goodput flat**: discarding work above the tipping point prevents the system from collapsing into unproductive thrash
- **Fairness prevents noisy-neighbor cascades**: per-customer quotas and rate limits keep one customer's spike from degrading service for everyone else
- **Constant work eliminates variability when it matters most**: systems doing the same operations regardless of state don't slow down during failures
- **Smaller service in control prevents stampedes**: when fleets are asymmetric, letting the smaller fleet set the pace prevents overload that would drop goodput to zero
- **Circuit breakers need granular fault domains**: aligning them to the dependency's actual partition boundaries prevents amplifying small failures into large ones
- **Adaptive retries balance availability and amplification**: retry quotas and client-side rate limits prevent retry storms from overwhelming impaired dependencies
- **Observability requires diverse perspectives and precise dimensions**: per-API, per-AZ, per-customer metrics catch problems that aggregate metrics hide
- **Proactive capacity management is the primary defense**: these patterns are safety nets for the exceptional cases, not primary operating modes
</KeyTakeaways>
