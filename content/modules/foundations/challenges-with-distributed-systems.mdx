## Introduction

Distributed systems are fundamental to modern cloud computing, yet they present unique challenges that differ significantly from traditional single-machine programming. At Amazon, the journey into distributed computing began in 1999 with just a handful of servers that could be given names like "fishy" or "online-01." Even with this modest infrastructure, distributed computing was far from easy. The challenges involved latency, scaling, understanding networking APIs, marshalling and unmarshalling data, and implementing complex algorithms like Paxos.

As systems quickly grew larger and more distributed, what had been theoretical edge cases turned into regular occurrences. Today, AWS services handle billions of requests across hundreds of thousands of machines, and the principles learned from over two decades of experience continue to shape how we build resilient systems.

The moment you add a second server to your infrastructure, you enter what can only be described as the "bizarre" world of distributed systems. This world operates according to different rules than the familiar territory of single-machine computing. Developing distributed utility computing services—whether reliable long-distance telephone networks or AWS services—is genuinely hard. Distributed computing is **weirder and less intuitive** than other forms of computing because of two interrelated problems: **independent failures** and **nondeterminism**.

Understanding these differences is essential for anyone building cloud-native applications, microservices, or any system that communicates across network boundaries. This lesson explores why distributed systems are fundamentally "weird," examines the failure modes that make them challenging, and introduces the core concepts that AWS uses to build reliable distributed systems.

## Types of Distributed Systems: Understanding the Difficulty Spectrum

Distributed systems actually vary significantly in difficulty of implementation. They exist on a spectrum, from relatively straightforward to extraordinarily complex. Understanding where your system falls on this spectrum helps you make appropriate design choices and set realistic expectations.

### Offline Distributed Systems (Easiest)

At one end of the spectrum, we have **offline distributed systems**. These include batch processing systems, big data analysis clusters, movie scene rendering farms, and protein folding clusters. While far from trivial to implement, offline distributed systems get almost all the benefits of distributed computing—**scalability and fault tolerance**—and almost none of the downsides—**complex failure modes and non-determinism**.

Why are offline systems easier? Because they don't have strict real-time requirements. If a batch job takes a few extra minutes because one node failed and work needed to be redistributed, it's usually acceptable. Results can be generated "eventually" rather than immediately.

### Soft Real-Time Distributed Systems (Moderate Difficulty)

In the middle of the spectrum, we have **soft real-time distributed systems**. These are critical systems that must continually produce or update results, but they have a relatively generous time window in which to do so.

Examples include:
- **Search index builders** that might be offline for 10 minutes to many hours without undue customer impact
- **Systems that look for impaired servers** in Amazon EC2
- **Credential rotation systems** for Amazon EC2 instances that must push updated credentials to essentially every EC2 instance, but have hours to do so because old credentials don't expire immediately

These systems must keep running and producing results, but short delays or temporary inconsistencies can be tolerated without immediate customer impact.

### Hard Real-Time Distributed Systems (Most Difficult)

At the far, and most difficult, end of the spectrum, we have **hard real-time distributed systems**. These are often called **request/reply services**. At Amazon, when we think about building a distributed system, the hard real-time system is the first type that comes to mind.

Unfortunately, hard real-time distributed systems are the most difficult to get right. What makes them difficult is that **requests arrive unpredictably and responses must be given rapidly**—for example, the customer is actively waiting for the response with a timeout measured in milliseconds or seconds.

Examples include:
- Front-end web servers handling user interactions
- The order pipeline processing purchases
- Credit card transaction processing
- Every AWS API (EC2, S3, Lambda, etc.)
- Telephony systems
- Any service where a human or application is synchronously waiting for a response

<Callout type="warning">
Hard real-time distributed systems are the major focus of this article and the primary challenge AWS faces. They require handling all the complexity of distributed computing while meeting strict latency and availability requirements.
</Callout>

Think carefully about whether you truly need hard real-time guarantees. Can your workload be restructured as batch processing or soft real-time? Moving down the difficulty spectrum can dramatically simplify your architecture and improve reliability. Event-driven architectures, message queues, and asynchronous processing can sometimes transform a hard real-time problem into a soft real-time one.

## Why Hard Real-Time Systems Are Fundamentally Weird

In the Superman comic books, Superman encounters an alter ego named Bizarro who lives on a planet called Bizarro World where everything is backwards. Bizarro looks kind of similar to Superman, but he is actually evil. Hard real-time distributed systems are the same—they look kind of like regular computing, but are actually different, and frankly, a bit on the evil side.

Hard real-time distributed systems development is bizarre for one reason: **request/reply networking**. We're not talking about the nitty-gritty details of TCP/IP, DNS, sockets, or other such protocols. Those subjects are potentially difficult to understand, but they resemble other hard problems in computing that can be mastered with study and practice.

What makes hard real-time distributed systems difficult is that **the network enables sending messages from one fault domain to another**. Sending a message might seem innocuous—just a function call across a network. In fact, sending messages is where everything starts getting more complicated than normal.

## Understanding Request/Reply Through Pac-Man

To understand the complexity, let's look at a simple example from an implementation of Pac-Man. Intended to run on a single machine, this code doesn't send any messages over any network:

```python
board.move(pacman, user.joystickDirection())
ghosts = board.findAll(":ghost")
for ghost in ghosts:
    if board.overlaps(pacman, ghost):
        user.slayBy(":ghost")
        board.remove(pacman)
        return
```

This is straightforward single-machine code. Each method call is a simple function invocation in the same process space. If the code fails, the entire machine has probably failed, and you can't handle that error anyway.

Now, let's imagine developing a networked version of this code, where the board object's state is maintained on a separate server. Every call to the board object, such as `findAll()`, results in sending and receiving messages between two servers. A simple expression becomes a complex network interaction.

## The Eight Failure Modes of Request/Reply Communication

Whenever a request/reply message is sent between two servers, **the same set of eight steps, at a minimum, must always happen**. There is no way to skip any of them. Each step can fail independently.

<Mermaid title="Client-Network-Server Message Exchange" chart={`sequenceDiagram
    participant CLIENT
    participant NETWORK
    participant SERVER

    Note over CLIENT: 1. POST REQUEST
    CLIENT->>NETWORK: Send MESSAGE
    Note over NETWORK: 2. DELIVER REQUEST
    NETWORK->>SERVER: Deliver MESSAGE
    Note over SERVER: 3. VALIDATE REQUEST
    Note over SERVER: 4. UPDATE SERVER STATE
    Note over SERVER: 5. POST REPLY
    SERVER->>NETWORK: Send REPLY
    Note over NETWORK: 6. DELIVER REPLY
    NETWORK->>CLIENT: Deliver REPLY
    Note over CLIENT: 7. VALIDATE REPLY
    Note over CLIENT: 8. UPDATE CLIENT STATE
`} />

Let's examine each step in detail:

### Step 1: POST REQUEST
CLIENT puts request MESSAGE onto NETWORK. Physically, this means sending packets via a network adapter, causing electrical signals to travel over wires through routers that comprise the network between CLIENT and SERVER. This step is separate from delivery because CLIENT might successfully transmit but NETWORK could lose packets.

### Step 2: DELIVER REQUEST
NETWORK delivers MESSAGE to SERVER. This could fail for independent reasons—SERVER might suddenly lose power and be unable to accept incoming packets, or intermediate routers might crash.

### Step 3: VALIDATE REQUEST
SERVER validates MESSAGE. This ensures the message is well-formed, authorized, and understandable. The cause of failure can be almost anything: corrupted packets, incompatible software versions, or bugs on either client or server.

### Step 4: UPDATE SERVER STATE
SERVER updates its state, if necessary, based on MESSAGE. This is the actual work being requested. It might fail due to resource constraints, data validation errors, or business logic conditions.

### Step 5: POST REPLY
SERVER puts reply REPLY onto NETWORK. Regardless of whether it's trying to reply with success or failure, SERVER could fail to post the reply—for example, its network card might fail at just the wrong moment.

### Step 6: DELIVER REPLY
NETWORK delivers REPLY to CLIENT. NETWORK could fail to deliver REPLY even though it successfully delivered MESSAGE in an earlier step. Networks are not transactional.

### Step 7: VALIDATE REPLY
CLIENT validates REPLY. The reply might be corrupted, incompatible with the client's expectations, or malformed.

### Step 8: UPDATE CLIENT STATE
CLIENT updates its state, if necessary, based on REPLY. The client might fail to understand the message due to incompatibility or fail for some other reason.

<Callout type="info">
A single request/reply over the network **explodes one thing (calling a method) into eight things**. Worse, CLIENT, SERVER, and NETWORK can fail independently from each other. Engineers' code must handle any of these steps failing.
</Callout>

## Fate Sharing: Why Single-Machine Code Is Simpler

Let's review this expression from the single-machine version of the code:

```python
board.find("pacman")
```

Technically, there are some weird ways this code could fail at runtime, even if the implementation is bug-free:
- The CPU could spontaneously overheat
- The machine's power supply could fail
- The kernel could panic
- Memory could fill up, preventing object creation
- The disk could fill up, preventing statistics logging
- A gamma ray could flip a bit in RAM (cosmic ray bit flip)

But most of the time, engineers don't worry about these things. Unit tests never cover the "what if the CPU fails" scenario, and only rarely cover out-of-memory scenarios.

Why? Because these failures occur on a single machine—a single **fault domain**. If the `board.find` method fails because the CPU spontaneously fries, it's safe to assume that the entire machine is down. It's not even conceptually possible to handle that error. Similar assumptions can be made about the other types of errors. You could try to write tests for some of these cases, but there is little point for typical engineering.

If these failures do happen, it's safe to assume that everything else will fail too. Technically, we say that they all **share fate**. Fate sharing cuts down immensely on the different failure modes that an engineer has to handle.

In contrast, **servers and the network do not share fate**. Unlike the single machine case:
- If the network fails, the client machine will keep working
- If the remote machine fails, the client machine will keep working
- If the client machine fails, the server keeps working

Each component can fail independently, and the others must handle that failure gracefully.

## Handling Failure Modes in Hard Real-Time Distributed Systems

Engineers working on hard real-time distributed systems must test for all aspects of network failure because the servers and the network do not share fate. Let's look at what happens when each of the eight steps fails:

### 1. POST REQUEST Fails
Either network NETWORK failed to deliver the message (for example, intermediate router crashed at just the wrong moment), or SERVER rejected it explicitly. This is the one case where the client knows deterministically that the request could not possibly have been received by the server machine.

### 2. DELIVER REQUEST Fails
NETWORK successfully delivers MESSAGE to SERVER, but SERVER crashes right after it receives MESSAGE but before processing it.

### 3. VALIDATE REQUEST Fails
SERVER decides that MESSAGE is invalid. The cause can be almost anything: corrupted packets, incompatible software versions, authentication failures, or bugs on either client or server.

### 4. UPDATE SERVER STATE Fails
SERVER tries to update its state, but it doesn't work. Perhaps a database is down, a disk is full, or business logic validation fails.

### 5. POST REPLY Fails
Regardless of whether it was trying to reply with success or failure, SERVER could fail to post the reply. For example, its network card might fail just at the wrong moment, or a process might crash before sending the response.

### 6. DELIVER REPLY Fails
NETWORK could fail to deliver REPLY to CLIENT even though NETWORK was working in earlier steps. Intermediate network equipment could fail, routes could change, or packets could be dropped due to congestion.

### 7. VALIDATE REPLY Fails
CLIENT decides that REPLY is invalid, perhaps due to corruption, incompatibility, or unexpected format.

### 8. UPDATE CLIENT STATE Fails
CLIENT could receive message REPLY but fail to update its own state, fail to understand the message (due to being incompatible), or fail for some other reason like out of memory.

These failure modes are what make distributed computing so hard. I call them **the eight failure modes of the apocalypse**.

## Expanding One Expression Into Fifteen Steps

In light of these failure modes, let's review this expression from the Pac-Man code again:

```python
board.find("pacman")
```

This simple expression expands into the following **client-side activities**:

1. Post a message, such as `{action: "find", name: "pacman", userId: "8765309"}`, onto the network, addressed to the Board machine.

2. If the network is unavailable, or the connection to the Board machine is explicitly refused, raise an error. This case is somewhat special because the client knows, deterministically, that the request could not possibly have been received by the server machine.

3. Wait for a reply, with a timeout.

4. If a reply is never received, time out. In this step, **timing out means that the result of the request is UNKNOWN**. It may or may not have happened. The client must handle UNKNOWN correctly.

5. If a reply is received, determine if it's a success reply, error reply, or incomprehensible/corrupt reply.

6. If it isn't an error, unmarshall the response and turn it into an object the code can understand.

7. If it is an error or incomprehensible reply, raise an exception.

8. Whatever handles the exception has to determine if it should retry the request or give up and stop the game.

The expression also starts the following **server-side activities**:

1. Receive the request (this may not happen at all).

2. Validate the request.

3. Look up the user to see if the user is still alive. (The server might have given up on the user because it hadn't received any messages from them for too long.)

4. Update the keep-alive table for the user so the server knows they're (probably) still there.

5. Look up the user's position.

6. Post a response containing something like `{xPos: 23, yPos: 92, clock: 23481984134}`.

7. Any further server logic must correctly handle the future effects of the client failing to receive the message, receiving it but not understanding it, receiving it and crashing, or handling it successfully.

**In summary, one expression in normal code turns into fifteen extra steps in hard real-time distributed systems code.** This expansion is due to the eight different points at which each round-trip communication between client and server can fail.

## The Code That Must Be Written

Any expression that represents a round trip over the network results in code that looks like this:

```python
(error, reply) = network.send(remote, actionData)

switch error:
    case POST_FAILED:
        # Handle case where you know server didn't get it
        # This is deterministic - safe to retry
        pass

    case RETRYABLE:
        # Handle case where server got it but reported transient failure
        # Use exponential backoff, respect retry budgets
        pass

    case FATAL:
        # Handle case where server got it and definitely doesn't like it
        # Don't retry - fix the request or give up
        pass

    case UNKNOWN:  # i.e., timeout
        # Handle case where the *only* thing you know is that the server
        # received the message; it may have been trying to report
        # SUCCESS, FATAL, or RETRYABLE
        # This is the hardest case
        pass

    case SUCCESS:
        if validate(reply):
            # Do something with reply object
            pass
        else:
            # Handle case where reply is corrupt/incompatible
            pass
```

**This complexity is unavoidable.** If code doesn't handle all cases correctly, the service will eventually fail in bizarre ways. Imagine trying to write tests for all the failure modes a client/server system such as the Pac-Man example could run into!

<Callout type="warning">
Every single network call in your distributed system must include error handling for all five outcomes: POST_FAILED, RETRYABLE, FATAL, UNKNOWN, and SUCCESS. Omitting any of these cases creates latent bugs that will emerge under rare conditions.
</Callout>

## The UNKNOWN Problem: Distributed Systems' Greatest Mystery

Perhaps the hardest thing to handle is the **UNKNOWN** error type. The client doesn't always know if the request succeeded. Maybe it did move Pac-Man (or, in a banking service, withdraw money from the user's bank account), or maybe it didn't. How should engineers handle such things? It's difficult because engineers are human, and humans tend to struggle with true uncertainty.

Humans are used to looking at code like this:

```python
def isEven(number):
    if number % 2 == 0:
        return True
    else:
        return False
```

Humans understand this code because it does what it looks like it does. The behavior is deterministic and predictable.

Humans struggle with the distributed version of the code:

```python
def distributedIsEven(number):
    result = mathServer.mod(number, 2)
    if result == 0:
        return True
    elif result == 1:
        return False
    elif result == UNKNOWN:
        return ???  # What the heck do we return here?
```

It's almost impossible for a human to figure out how to handle UNKNOWN correctly. What does UNKNOWN really mean? Should the code retry? If so, how many times? How long should it wait between retries?

### The UNKNOWN Problem With Side Effects

It gets even worse when code has side-effects. Inside of a budgeting application running on a single machine, withdrawing money from an account is straightforward:

```python
class Teller:
    def doWithdraw(self, account, amount):
        result = account.withdraw(amount)
        if result == SUCCESS:
            return True
        elif result == INSUFFICIENT_FUNDS:
            return False
```

However, the distributed version of that application is weird because of UNKNOWN:

```python
class DistributedTeller:
    def doWithdraw(self, account, amount):
        result = self.accountService.withdraw(account, amount)
        if result == SUCCESS:
            return True
        elif result == INSUFFICIENT_FUNDS:
            return False
        elif result == UNKNOWN:
            # Did the money get withdrawn?
            # Was the account debited?
            # Should we retry (risking double withdrawal)?
            # Should we return failure (possibly lying to customer)?
            return ???
```

> "It's almost impossible for a human to figure out how to handle UNKNOWN correctly. What does UNKNOWN really mean? Should the code retry? If so, how many times? How long should it wait between retries?"

This is why concepts like **idempotency** (operations that can be safely retried with the same result), **idempotency tokens** (unique identifiers that prevent duplicate processing), and **exactly-once semantics** are so important in distributed systems. They help mitigate the UNKNOWN problem by making retries safe.

## Testing Hard Real-Time Distributed Systems: The Explosion of Complexity

Testing the single-machine version of the Pac-Man code snippet is comparatively straightforward. Create some different Board objects, put them into different states, create some User objects in different states, and so forth. Engineers would think hardest about edge conditions, and maybe use generative testing or a fuzzer.

In the Pac-Man code, there are four places where the board object is used. In distributed Pac-Man, there are four points in that code that have **five different possible outcomes** (POST_FAILED, RETRYABLE, FATAL, UNKNOWN, or SUCCESS).

These multiply the state space of tests tremendously. Engineers of hard real-time distributed systems have to handle many permutations:
- Say that the call to `board.find()` fails with POST_FAILED
- Then test what happens when it fails with RETRYABLE
- Then test what happens if it fails with FATAL
- Then test what happens if it fails with UNKNOWN
- And so on for all four calls to board

Let's say an engineer came up with 10 scenarios to test in the single-machine version of Pac-Man. But, in the distributed systems version, they have to test each of those scenarios 20 times (5 outcomes × 4 network calls). **The test matrix balloons from 10 to 200!**

### Testing Server-Side Interactions

But, wait, there's more. The engineer may also own the server code. Whatever combination of client, network, and server side errors occur, they must test so that the client and the server don't end up in a corrupted state.

The server code might look like this:

```python
def handleFind(channel, message):
    if not validate(message):
        channel.send(INVALID_MESSAGE)
        return

    if not userThrottle.ok(message.user()):
        channel.send(RETRYABLE_ERROR)
        return

    location = database.lookup(message.user())
    if location.error():
        channel.send(USER_NOT_FOUND)
        return
    else:
        channel.send(SUCCESS, location)

def handleMove(...):
    # Similar complexity

def handleFindAll(...):
    # Similar complexity

def handleRemove(...):
    # Similar complexity
```

There are four server-side functions to test. Let's assume that each function, on a single machine, has five tests each. That's 20 tests right there.

Because clients send multiple messages to the same server, tests should simulate sequences of different requests to make sure that the server remains robust. Let's say one construct has 10 different scenarios with an average of three calls in each scenario. That's 30 more tests.

But, one scenario also needs to test failure cases. For each of those tests, you need to simulate what happens if the client received any of the four failure types (POST_FAILED, RETRYABLE, FATAL, and UNKNOWN) and then calls the server again with an invalid request.

For example, a client might successfully call `find`, but then get UNKNOWN back when it calls `move`. It might then call `find` again for some reason. Does the server handle this case correctly? Probably, but you won't know unless you test for it.

**As with the client-side code, the test matrix on the server side explodes in complexity as well.**

<Callout type="info">
In practice, AWS services use a combination of strategies to manage this testing complexity:
- Chaos engineering to inject failures randomly
- Property-based testing to generate test cases automatically
- Simulation frameworks that can replay production traffic patterns
- Extensive integration testing in pre-production environments
</Callout>

## Handling Unknown Unknowns: Distrust Everything

It is mind-boggling to consider all the permutations of failures that a distributed system can encounter, especially over multiple requests. One way we've found to approach distributed engineering is to **distrust everything**. Every line of code, unless it could not possibly cause network communication, might not do what it's supposed to.

This defensive mindset leads to practices like:
- Always having timeouts on network calls (never infinite waits)
- Always validating inputs, even from "trusted" internal services
- Always handling all error cases, even "impossible" ones
- Always logging enough information to debug unexpected conditions
- Always measuring and monitoring actual behavior, not assumed behavior

Think about what you're measuring. Where in the system are you measuring it? What are you not measuring that could go wrong?

## Recursive Distribution: Complexity at Every Level

The eight failure modes of the apocalypse can happen **at any level of abstraction** within a distributed system. The earlier example was limited to a single client machine, a network, and a single server machine. Even in that simplistic scenario, the failure state matrix exploded in complexity.

Real distributed systems have more complicated failure state matrices than the single client machine example. Real distributed systems consist of multiple machines that may be viewed at **multiple levels of abstraction**:

1. Individual machines
2. Groups of machines (like those in an Availability Zone)
3. Groups of groups of machines (like those in a Region)
4. And so on, potentially

For example, a service built on AWS might group together machines dedicated to handling resources within a particular Availability Zone. There might be two more groups of machines that handle two other Availability Zones. Then, those groups might be grouped into an AWS Region group. And that Region group might communicate (logically) with other Region groups.

Unfortunately, **even at this higher, more logical level, all the same problems apply**.

### Group-Level Request/Reply

Let's assume a service has grouped some servers into a single logical group, GROUP1. Group GROUP1 might sometimes send messages to another group of servers, GROUP2. This is an example of **recursive distributed engineering**.

All the same networking failure modes described earlier can apply here. Say that GROUP1 wants to send a request to GROUP2:

One way or another, some machine within GROUP1 has to put a message on the network, addressed (logically) to GROUP2. Some machine within GROUP2 has to process the request, and so forth.

The fact that GROUP1 and GROUP2 are comprised of groups of machines doesn't change the fundamentals. **GROUP1, GROUP2, and NETWORK can still fail independently of each other.**

### Machine-Level Recursion Within Groups

However, that is just the group-level view. There is also **machine-to-machine level interaction within each group**. For example, GROUP2 might be structured with internal replication:

<Mermaid title="Server Replication for Durability" chart={`graph TD

At first, a message to GROUP2 is sent via the load balancer to one machine (possibly S20) within the group. The designers of the system know that S20 might fail during the UPDATE STATE phase. As a result, S20 may need to pass the message to at least one other machine, either one of its peers or a machine in a different group.

How does S20 actually do this? **By sending a request/reply message to, say, S25.** Thus, S20 is performing networking recursively. All the same eight failures can occur, independently, again.

Distributed engineering is happening twice, instead of once:
- The GROUP1 to GROUP2 message can fail in all eight ways
- The S20 to S25 message can itself fail, independently, in all eight ways

Testing this scenario would involve, at minimum:
- A test for all eight ways GROUP1 to GROUP2 group-level messaging can fail
- A test for all eight ways S20 to S25 server-level messaging can fail
- Tests for combinations of failures at both levels occurring simultaneously

This request/reply messaging example shows why **testing distributed systems remains an especially vexing problem**, even after over 20 years of experience with them. Testing is challenging given the vastness of edge cases, but it's especially important in these systems. Bugs can take a long time to surface after systems are deployed, and bugs can have an unpredictably broad impact to a system and its adjacent systems.

## Distributed Bugs Are Often Latent

If a failure is going to happen eventually, common wisdom is that it's better if it happens sooner rather than later. For example, it's better to find out about a scaling problem in a service, which will require six months to fix, at least six months before that service will have to achieve such scale.

Likewise, it's better to find bugs before they hit production. If the bugs do hit production, it's better to find them quickly, before they affect many customers or have other adverse effects.

**Distributed bugs**—meaning, those resulting from failing to handle all the permutations of eight failure modes of the apocalypse—are often severe. Examples over time abound in large distributed systems, from telecommunications systems to core internet systems.

Not only are these outages widespread and expensive, they can be **caused by bugs that were deployed to production months earlier**. It then takes a while to trigger the combination of scenarios that actually lead to these bugs happening (and spreading across the entire system).

Consider a bug in error handling code that is only triggered when:
1. A specific API call fails with a specific error code
2. AND the retry logic times out
3. AND the system is under moderate load (not low, not high)
4. AND a secondary dependency is also experiencing issues

This combination might not occur for months after deployment, even though the bug has been in production the entire time. When it finally does occur, the bug can cause widespread impact because it affects the network-based error handling that ties systems together.

## Distributed Bugs Spread Epidemically

Let me describe another problem that is fundamental to distributed bugs:

1. **Distributed bugs necessarily involve use of the network**
2. Therefore, **distributed bugs are more likely to spread to other machines** (or groups of machines), because, by definition, they already involve the only thing that links machines together

Amazon has experienced these distributed bugs, too. An old, but relevant, example is a site-wide failure of www.amazon.com. The failure was caused by a single server failing within the remote catalog service when its disk filled up.

Due to mishandling of that error condition, the remote catalog server started returning empty responses to every request it received. It also started returning them **very quickly**, because it's a lot faster to return nothing than something (at least it was in this case).

Meanwhile, the load balancer between the website and the remote catalog service didn't notice that all the responses were zero-length. But, it did notice that they were **blazingly faster** than all the other remote catalog servers.

So, it sent a huge amount of the traffic from www.amazon.com to the one remote catalog server whose disk was full. Effectively, the entire website went down because one remote server couldn't display any product information.

We found the bad server quickly and removed it from service to restore the website. Then, we followed up with our usual process of determining root causes and identifying issues to prevent the situation from happening again. We shared those lessons across Amazon to help prevent other systems from having the same problem.

In addition to learning the specific lessons about this failure mode, this incident served as a great example of how **failure modes propagate quickly and unpredictably in distributed systems**.

<Callout type="warning">
A bug in a single machine can affect thousands of machines through the network. Always consider: If this component fails, what will happen to the systems that depend on it? Will they handle the failure gracefully, or will they amplify the problem?
</Callout>

## Summary of Problems in Distributed Systems

In short, engineering for distributed systems is hard because:

- **Engineers can't combine error conditions.** Instead, they must consider many permutations of failures. Most errors can happen at any time, independently of (and therefore, potentially, in combination with) any other error condition.

- **The result of any network operation can be UNKNOWN**, in which case the request may have succeeded, failed, or received but not processed. Handling UNKNOWN correctly is one of the hardest problems in distributed systems.

- **Distributed problems occur at all logical levels** of a distributed system, not just low-level physical machines. The same challenges exist whether you're thinking about individual servers, groups of servers in an AZ, or groups of AZs in a Region.

- **Distributed problems get worse at higher levels of the system, due to recursion.** Each level of abstraction inherits all the complexity of lower levels plus adds its own.

- **Distributed bugs often show up long after they are deployed to a system.** Rare combinations of conditions might not occur for months, making these bugs extremely difficult to find through testing.

- **Distributed bugs can spread across an entire system.** Because they involve the network by definition, they have the mechanism to propagate rapidly.

- **Many of the above problems derive from the laws of physics of networking, which can't be changed.** No amount of clever engineering can eliminate the fundamental challenges of distributed systems—we can only learn to manage them better.

## Strategies for Tackling Distributed Systems Challenges

Just because distributed computing is hard—and weird—doesn't mean that there aren't ways to tackle these problems. Throughout the Amazon Builders' Library, we dig into how AWS manages distributed systems. Some key strategies include:

**Idempotency and Idempotency Tokens** – Making operations safe to retry by ensuring they have the same effect whether executed once or multiple times. Idempotency tokens help track requests and prevent duplicate processing.

**Exponential Backoff and Jitter** – Retrying failed requests with increasing delays and random jitter to avoid thundering herds and give systems time to recover.

**Circuit Breakers** – Detecting failing dependencies and stopping requests to them temporarily, giving them time to recover and preventing cascade failures.

**Bulkheads and Fault Isolation** – Isolating failures to specific components or customer segments so one failure doesn't bring down the entire system.

**Timeout Design** – Setting appropriate timeouts at every level and ensuring timeout values are coordinated across service boundaries.

**Observability and Monitoring** – Measuring actual behavior comprehensively to detect unexpected conditions quickly.

**Testing Strategies** – Using chaos engineering, fault injection, load testing, and simulation to find bugs before production.

**Design Patterns** – Employing proven patterns like sagas for distributed transactions, CRDTs for conflict-free replication, and consensus algorithms for coordination.

<KeyTakeaways>
- **Request/reply messaging has eight distinct steps that can each fail independently**: POST REQUEST, DELIVER REQUEST, VALIDATE REQUEST, UPDATE SERVER STATE, POST REPLY, DELIVER REPLY, VALIDATE REPLY, UPDATE CLIENT STATE—creating a combinatorial explosion of failure scenarios
- **UNKNOWN is the hardest error type to handle correctly**: When requests timeout, you genuinely don't know if they succeeded, requiring idempotency, idempotency tokens, or other mechanisms to make retries safe
- **Testing complexity explodes exponentially in distributed systems**: A simple function with 10 test cases in single-machine code might require 200+ tests when distributed, and server-side testing adds even more permutations
- **Distributed bugs are latent and epidemic**: They can remain dormant for months after deployment until rare conditions trigger them, then spread rapidly across systems through network connections
- **Recursion multiplies complexity at every level**: The same eight failure modes apply whether you're thinking about individual machines, groups of machines in an AZ, or groups of AZs in a Region—and failures at each level can combine
- **Fate sharing doesn't exist across networks**: Unlike single-machine code where component failures mean total system failure, distributed systems must handle independent failures of clients, servers, and networks
- **The difficulty spectrum matters**: Offline and soft real-time systems are significantly easier than hard real-time request/reply services—consider whether you truly need hard real-time guarantees before committing to that architecture
- **Many problems derive from physics**: Network latency, independent failures, and nondeterminism are fundamental properties that no amount of clever engineering can eliminate—only manage better
